LORE Crawl
In the Age of Continuity, beneath ash-choked heavens and the endless shadow of the great Turris-Spireholds, humanity endures upon the edge of extinction.
Long ago, Immog Aeter, the First Distiller, defied the dominion of death and forged the foundations of the Cineric Concord from war, ash, and the rendered remains of the dead beyond the veil. Yet the victory was never complete. The Cinerum still hungers.
The worlds of mankind have withered beneath unending strain. Ancient systems decay even as the great manufactories roar without pause. Across a thousand dead stars the Concordia Logistica counts the living, the dying, and the already claimed. In the upper towers the Ashbourne preserve themselves through stolen years while beneath them uncounted billions labor in darkness so the species may survive one century longer.
Still the enemy comes.
The Conceptuals stir within the Ashen Sea. The dead whisper through breach zones and forgotten vaults. The cults of Zaltech preach strength through consumption while the scream of Nezram echoes through the minds of the grieving. Beyond the mapped void drift ancient sovereignties of extinct gods, hollow saints, flesh kingdoms, and things that wear memory like skin.
Against these horrors stand the Eternus Legio.
Upon fortress worlds, breach frontiers, and burning Turris depths, the Cohortes Ferrata and the Legio Linea wage endless war beside ordinary citizens pressed into service by necessity and survival alike. Mortals fight the deathless in corridors of steel and ash while Custodia Cineris kill what should not exist and the Collegia Mortis bind shut the wounds between worlds.
Courage has not vanished from mankind.
It survives in ration lines beneath bombardment.
In the hands of furnace workers who continue their labor while the dead scratch at the walls.
In the soldiers who descend into the Undertiers knowing they may return speaking with borrowed voices.
In the void crews who guide black-hulled vessels through the shifting Deadward tides between stars.
For interstellar travel itself is an act of terror.
The great void engines of the Concord pass through regions where reality thins and the pressure of the Cinerum bleeds against the material world. Entire crews vanish between systems. Choirs hear singing in sealed compartments. Some ships emerge decades late. Others arrive carrying passengers who died before departure.
Yet still the ships travel.
Because without them the towers starve.
Without them the furnaces die.
Without them humanity stands alone beneath the hunger of the dead.
Thus the Concord endures:
not through purity,
nor hope,
nor mercy,
but through iron continuity, furnace law, military violence, and the terrible refusal to surrender the living to the ash.
