## DOCTRINAL NOTICE OF CLASSIFICATION

The following record concerns the Nomenclature Ledger, the sanctioned-name rolls, and the sixty-four digit ident registers by which the Concord preserves civic continuity where language has failed.

Common speech calls this office by several names.

The Name Halls.

The Dead List.

The Number Room.

The Ledger of Faces.

The Bureau of Too Many Johns.

The Grave Alphabet.

Among poor districts, it is more often called the place where one's child becomes a code.

Among noble houses, it is called the office that prevents the wrong heir from being remembered.

Among Logistica factors, it is called a throughput necessity.

Among Null Court judges, it is called an instrument of lawful disambiguation.

Among the Collegia Mortis, it is called an apology issued too late.

Names were never designed to bear the weight of eternity.

They were made for families, villages, ships, shrines, bloodlines, farms, trades, streets, patronymics, devotional vows, and the small arrogant hope that no household would ever need to distinguish its dead from all the dead mankind had ever produced.

Then humanity lived too long.

Then humanity multiplied too widely.

Then humanity carried its names to the stars.

Then it lost the stars and found them again through ash.

Then it began dying in numbers so vast that memory itself became a clerical hazard.

By the time the Cineric Concord inherited the ruins of older lexigraphic systems, the registers were already polluted beyond repair. First names had been reused across eons. Middle names had migrated through devotional fashion, family mimicry, military admiration, cult concealment, and noble affectation. Last names had splintered, merged, been sold, granted, revoked, resurrected, translated, mistranslated, shortened by famine clerks, lengthened by pride, and duplicated by whole districts attempting to hide inside respectable syllables.

The Collegia Mortis therefore records the central truth of all civic identity in the Continuity age:

A name is not an identifier.

A name is an address for grief.

A number is the identifier.

The living dislike this doctrine.

The dead have never successfully objected.

## THE FAILURE OF HUMAN NAMING

Human naming failed long before humanity reached the great dark.

This is not a statement of moral failure. It is an arithmetical postmortem.

Ancient municipal ledgers, pre-stellar census tablets, maritime crew rolls, burial plates, ration tallies, slave emancipations, debtor writs, tax rolls, military muster books, devotional registers, hospital admissions, adoption warrants, criminal warrants, wedding licenses, monastery rolls, guild rosters, penal manifests, and the private vanity archives of forgotten dynasts all display the same primitive confidence.

They believed a person could be found by a name.

They believed the addition of a parent, a birthplace, a trade, a spouse, a date, a local number, or a clerk's notation would save the register from confusion.

For a time, in small places, it did.

The Concord is not a small place.

The empire is not even a place in the older sense. It is a bruise of habitation across inhabited worlds, dead worlds, ashward installations, spireholds, orbitals, shorelines, flotilla colonies, furnace provinces, manufactorum continents, hive strata, noble preserves, penal engines, mausoleum cities, and lost jurisdictions that still transmit tax petitions under seal marks no living clerk remembers issuing.

Within such a civilization, names repeat.

They repeat until repetition becomes geology.

A first name may belong to saints, murderers, mothers, corpse-haulers, distillers, famine clerks, soldiers, mad prophets, noble bastards, vat-bred laborers, foundlings, cloned retainers, cultists using stolen nursery names, and whole generations named after a victorious Legate by order of civic gratitude.

A middle name may mark a shrine oath on one world, a sewer caste on another, a rebel family in a splinter conclave, and a sanctioned mortuary dedication in a district that has been burned three times.

A last name may be a bloodline, a purchased title, a legal fiction, a company mark, a noble imitation, a punishment, a witness-protection alias, a dead language occupational residue, a transliteration error, or the surviving fragment of a family that no longer knows what it means.

The Nomenclature Ledger was not created because the Concord loves numbers.

It was created because names lied without intending to.

The current sanctioned rolls maintained under Mortis-Logistica concordance contain the following approved civil name pools for standard Concordial prefixing before ident enumeration:

**164 approved first names.**

**150 approved middle names.**

These figures are not symbolic.

They are operational constants.

They are repeated here under Collegia seal because future clerks, generator artisans, muniment scribes, Logistica auditors, and civic registry artificers are forbidden to pretend the sanctioned rolls are larger than they are merely because the human ear grows bored.

The sanctioned rolls are not intended to create uniqueness.

They are intended to create lawful pronounceability before the application of the ident.

A citizen's civil name is therefore a prefix, not a proof.

The approved first name establishes permitted ordinary address.

The approved middle name establishes secondary distinction, devotional flavor, family vanity, or local registry habit.

The approved last name establishes house, line, adoption, district, patronal carryover, civic grant, penal inheritance, or legally tolerated fiction.

The ident establishes the citizen.

Thus the correct legal sequence is:

**Sanctioned First Name + Sanctioned Middle Name + Sanctioned Last Name + Sixty-Four Digit Ident.**

Any office omitting the ident from binding instruments is not recording a citizen.

It is recording a rumor wearing a hat.

The Collegia Mortis recognizes only those rumors that can be burned, inherited, taxed, revived, autopsied, accused, or sealed to a number.

Illegal names are not merely rude names, foreign names, cult names, alien names, or names inconvenient to clerks.

Some are these things.

Most are more dangerous.

A name may be illegal because it resembles a sealed noble line closely enough to create succession fraud.

A name may be illegal because it was used by a Cineric cult as a binding phrase during a Black Tide event.

A name may be illegal because it appears in too many ash-zone testimonies without corresponding births.

A name may be illegal because ghosts respond to it.

A name may be illegal because a Null Court deleted it from permissible speech after an inheritance war.

A name may be illegal because a splinter conclave used it as a citizenship marker and the Legio does not permit enemy civic identity to propagate behind Concord lines.

A name may be illegal because a saint bore it, and ten million parents attempted to honor that saint in the same century, drowning three provincial registers under identical devotional filings.

A name may be illegal because a traitor bore it and the extermination writ was worded broadly.

The public is instructed that illegal names are forbidden for civic clarity.

This is true.

It is also incomplete enough to be considered kind.

## THE SIXTY-FOUR DIGIT IDENT

The modern Concordial ident is a unique integer registration identity composed of sixty-four digits derived from atomic time clock sequencing, local registry salt, Mortis witness counters, Logistica assignment hashes, and approved numerical scrambling rites.

The vulgar call it random.

The Logistica calls it reliably non-repeating under authorized issuance conditions.

The Collegia calls it the least romantic instrument ever invented to prevent a widow from burying the wrong husband twice.

A sixty-four digit ident is not a decorative code.

It is the binding civic spine of the person.

It attaches to the citizen while living.

It attaches to the corpse while dead.

It attaches to the remnant if the corpse speaks.

It attaches to the ash if the corpse burns improperly.

It attaches to rations, debts, sentences, pardons, conscriptions, surgical records, Draught exposure, noble service contracts, penal transfers, mortuary extractions, and military casualty reports.

It attaches to the legal fiction that the state knows who a person is.

No identifier is perfect.

The Collegia does not teach perfection.

It teaches survivable ambiguity.

The ident is not sacred because it cannot fail.

It is sacred because without it all failure becomes identical.

## WHY THE COLLEGIA MORTIS HOLDS THE NAME LEDGER

Many have asked why the management of names belongs partly to the Collegia Mortis rather than entirely to Concordia Logistica.

The answer is simple and has never satisfied anyone who asked it.

Names become most important when the body can no longer correct the clerk.

At birth, a name is hope.

At marriage, a name is negotiation.

At enlistment, a name is muster.

At sentencing, a name is liability.

At ration issue, a name is mouth.

At inheritance, a name is property.

At execution, a name is finality.

At burial, a name is mercy.

At resurrection, Reversion, revenance, possession, echoing, ghost testimony, memory plague, ash imitation, or noble continuance audit, a name becomes evidence in a hostile universe.

The Collegia Mortis therefore maintains death-side custody over nomenclature because the dead create the worst identity disputes.

A living fraud may be questioned.

A corpse may be opened.

A ghost may lie with the voice of someone beloved.

A noble Reversion may return wearing the name of the heir it consumed.

A Cineric echo may repeat the nursery name of a child who died before the district was founded.

A debt-ghost may claim an ident number to which it has no lawful hunger.

A name-eater may remove a citizen from every mouth that loved them while leaving the ident intact in ledgers no one can emotionally recognize.

The Collegia holds the ledger because death is where names go to be tested.

## REPETITION AS CIVIC HAZARD

Repetition is not merely inconvenient.

It kills.

The Mortis archives preserve multiple censored examples.

In the Dalmoran Ratification Spiral, three living citizens, two dead soldiers, a noble concubine, a penal corpse, and one improperly sealed ghost all shared the same first, middle, and last name across four jurisdictions. A pension intended for the soldier's widow was claimed by the concubine's estate, paid to a penal labor office, intercepted by a cult front, and finally attached to the ghost as evidence of continued personhood. The resulting probate dispute required two executions, one furnace denial, and the permanent sealing of a chapel that had begun praying to the wrong claimant.

In the Caul Trench Muster Error, a Legio company marched with seventeen soldiers bearing identical spoken names. Field officers, operating under artillery fog and ash interference, reassigned casualty tags by shouted surname. Three of the dead were recorded alive. Two living soldiers were declared dead and their rations terminated. One of the living men returned to find his wife remarried under Logistica approval and his child assigned to another household by famine court order. The man later joined a splinter banner under his own name, which by then legally belonged to a corpse.

In the Virellian Cradle Petition, an entire maternity ward named children after a local saint during a month of plague deliverance. The saint's name was approved. The mass devotion was legal. The result was ninety-two thousand near-identical registry entries within one provincial cycle. The Collegia required forty years to untangle mortuary obligations from the resulting name cluster. The Cultus Vitae still refuses to admit fault. The Logistica still refuses to admit anyone warned it in time. The Null Court has forbidden public performance of the saint's hymn within registry halls.

In the Del Monto aftermath, discussed under Null Court seal, obscure legal wording caused whole lower-stack strata to become entangled under a single civic name-form. That event is not treated here except to state the obvious Mortis lesson: if language alone defines citizenship, a bad sentence can manufacture a crowd wearing one face.

Names repeat.

Bodies do not always remain helpful enough to clarify.

## NAMED CITIZENS, NUMBERED CITIZENS, AND THE CRUEL MERCY OF BOTH

There is a persistent underclass complaint that the ident reduces a citizen to a number.

The Collegia Mortis does not dismiss the complaint.

It merely records that numbers have saved more poor citizens from clerical disappearance than noble poetry ever did.

A name can be stolen by a landlord.

A number is harder to steal without leaving marks.

A name can be mocked into silence.

A number persists in a ledger.

A name can be replaced by a foreman's nickname, a prison designation, a spirehold insult, a cult title, or a battlefield epithet.

A number can still call a ration entitlement back from a corrupt office if the office has not burned.

A name can be erased from family memory by plague, ash, displacement, fear, or the deliberate violence of those who prefer the poor to be interchangeable.

A number can be matched to dental remnants, marrow tags, blood salts, burn ash, birth plates, ration cards, debt nails, shrine offerings, and the hidden scratches mothers place inside children's boots when evacuation bells begin.

This is the cruelty of the ident.

It is also its mercy.

The Concord appends numbers because names have become too loved to be trusted and too reused to be lawful.

The citizen deserves a name because grief requires a sound.

The citizen requires a number because civilization requires continuity when grief cannot speak.

## NOBLE OBJECTIONS AND ASHBOURNE EXEMPTION FRAUD

The Ashbourne and their attendant noble houses have objected to universal ident attachment since the early Continuity stabilization edicts.

Their objections are old, ornate, expensive, and mostly defeated.

They claim bloodline should suffice.

The Collegia has autopsied too many false heirs to agree.

They claim house memory is stronger than public ledgering.

The Collegia has watched house memory change after a patriarch's third Reversion.

They claim the attachment of a number vulgarizes ancient names.

The Collegia notes that ancient names became ancient by surviving the vulgar.

They claim their private registries contain subtler distinctions than any Mortis or Logistica ledger.

The Collegia notes that private registries are often maintained by clerks paid to preserve the most profitable version of the truth.

Noble exemption fraud remains a persistent threat. False lineal continuance petitions frequently attempt to use antique name-forms, pre-Concord honorifics, dead planetary titles, devotional middle names, or obsolete military styles to imply legal uniqueness without submitting to ident verification.

This practice is forbidden.

It continues.

The Collegia tolerates noble vanity until it touches succession, Draught entitlement, Reversion priority, corpse ownership, or civic command authority.

At that point vanity becomes evidence.

Evidence can be weighed.

A weighed noble is never as heavy as they believe.

## CULT NAMES, ASH NAMES, AND THE DANGER OF UNAPPROVED ADDRESS

The Cinerum has never respected Concordial registries.

This discourtesy is administratively irritating and metaphysically expected.

Cineric cults often adopt ash names, borrowed names, name inversions, furnace epithets, post-mortem titles, communal names, or names recovered from the mouths of the dead. Such names are not merely aliases. In multiple recorded cult structures, name exchange served as initiation, identity dilution, sacrifice preparation, and doorway alignment.

The Collegia distinguishes among several forbidden categories:

Ash-borne names: names appearing first in residue, soot, furnace bloom, lung ash, or shoreline sediment.

Return names: names spoken by revenants, echoes, debt-ghosts, or grief apparitions before any matching birth can be found.

Predatory names: names that cause listeners to recall false acquaintances, false kinship, false debts, or false guilt.

Communal names: names used by groups to collapse individual liability into a single devotional or cultic persona.

Hunger names: names that increase in recorded frequency after deaths associated with famine, cannibal rites, Didam intrusion, or illegal Draught extraction.

Silence names: names that disappear from living memory while remaining legible in ledgers, often associated with name-eater activity.

The public is told to avoid illegal names because unauthorized naming can complicate registration.

This is again true.

It is also the sort of truth given to children because the adult version attracts attention from things that enjoy being named.

## POST-MORTEM NAMING AND THE RIGHT TO BE FOUND

A corpse without a name is a technical problem.

A corpse without an ident is an emergency.

The Collegia Mortis maintains that every recoverable human remnant must be reconciled to an ident whenever materially possible. Failure to do so creates administrative ghosts before metaphysical ones, and the metaphysical universe is opportunistic around paperwork.

Unidentified bodies accumulate resentment in the living.

Misidentified bodies accumulate legal rot.

Unnumbered bodies accumulate appetite.

The last statement is not poetic. It is based on furnace behavior, ash-seep response, and repeated breach-zone observation.

Human remains denied lawful attachment to prior identity are more susceptible to hostile reclassification by Cineric influence. They become easier for cults to claim, easier for memory plagues to wear, easier for grief predators to counterfeit, and easier for negligent offices to discard as surplus matter.

The ident therefore functions as a post-mortem anchor.

It says:

this was not merely meat.

this was not merely ash.

this was not merely a resource.

this belonged to an entry.

this entry belonged to law.

law has not yet admitted defeat.

## THE LEXIGRAPHIC POLLUTION OF THE OLD REGISTERS

The Collegia preserves pre-Concord lexigraphic register studies because they explain why reform failed before enumeration was adopted.

Old archivists attempted to solve repetition by adding more words.

They added birth-world.

They added district.

They added parental line.

They added patronymic.

They added matronymic.

They added caste.

They added occupation.

They added guild.

They added devotional mark.

They added military unit.

They added local seal.

They added a date.

Then calendars fractured.

They added a place.

Then places were renamed, bombarded, abandoned, moved, subsumed into spire growth, swallowed by ash, reclaimed under new charters, or declared never to have existed by embarrassed governors.

They added a witness.

Then witnesses died, lied, fled, ascended, reverted, forgot, duplicated testimony, or were found to be ghosts.

They added family.

Then families split, merged, sold children, adopted strangers, concealed mutants, purchased noble style, erased penal ancestry, and changed names to survive frontier purges.

They added language.

Then languages died.

They added machine translation.

Then the Heliun Divide taught them why entrusting identity to obedient machine minds had been a civilization-scale error.

By the time the Concord embraced sixty-four digit enumeration, the old registers were less archives than sediment layers of human panic.

The Nomenclature Ledger does not despise the old names.

It pities them.

They tried to do the work of numbers while still pretending the universe was small enough to remember a face.

## THE NAME AS SOCIAL MERCY

The Collegia Mortis is often accused of preferring numbers to names.

This accusation is wrong in detail and understandable in spirit.

The Collegia does not prefer numbers.

It distrusts comfort.

A name is comfort. It is the sound a mother uses. It is the last thing a soldier may hear. It is the word a lover writes inside a coat. It is the mark on a cup. It is the syllable by which a child learns that they are not the room, not the bed, not the ration line, not the ash outside the window, but a self called from the blur.

The Collegia knows this.

That is why names are dangerous.

The institutions that govern death must be most careful with the things the living cannot bear to examine.

A number alone is cruelty.

A name alone is chaos.

Together they form the smallest unit of survivable recognition.

The name lets the living mourn.

The ident lets the state find what mourning is pointed at.

Neither is sufficient.

Both are necessary.

## FINAL ARCHIVAL POSITION

The empire contains more dead than living.

It contains more records than citizens.

It contains more repeated names than any sane civilization would tolerate if sanity were still an available administrative posture.

The Concord therefore appends the citizen to a number because the human mouth cannot carry the burden alone.

This is not an insult to the person.

It is an admission that the person matters enough to require defense against duplication, theft, misfiling, ash imitation, noble fraud, cult absorption, battlefield confusion, inheritance hunger, and the long slow erosion by which empires forget the poor first.

Let the citizen have a name.

Let the family speak it.

Let the priest bless it.

Let the soldier shout it.

Let the lover carve it.

Let the mourner whisper it.

Then let the Ledger attach the ident, because love is not a filing system and grief has never once passed an audit unaided.

A name calls the dead home.

A number proves which door should open.
