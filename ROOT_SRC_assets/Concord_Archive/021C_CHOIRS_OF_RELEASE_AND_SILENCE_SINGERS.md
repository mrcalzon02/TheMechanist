The **Choirs of Release** use rhythm, breath, hum, whispered grief, and synchronized silence to lighten groups. They often emerge from mourning restrictions, factory work chants, prison call-and-response, or temple hymnody distorted by despair.

The **Silence Singers** are the more advanced and more dangerous branch. They produce no audible song. Members stand together and breathe in such alignment that bystanders begin matching them unconsciously. The first symptom is calm. The second is forgetting why one intended to leave.

### Formation

Sonic cells form where speech is already regulated. Districts under curfew, mourning bans, labor chants, or military cadence are vulnerable because the population has been trained to move emotion through rhythm. The Unburdened do not need to invent the channel. They only change its destination.

### Field Signs

Listen for songs whose lyrics vary but cadence remains fixed. Watch for crowds that inhale together before answering. Note whether infants stop crying when the chant begins. Check whether soldiers lower weapons in time with the breath cycle. Question anyone who says the silence feels full.

### Suppression

Do not deploy standard marching units into an active Release Choir unless they have been de-cadenced. The Choir will use discipline against them.
