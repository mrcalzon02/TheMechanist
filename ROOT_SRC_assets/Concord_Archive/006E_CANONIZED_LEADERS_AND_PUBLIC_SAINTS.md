## ON SAINTHOOD AS CIVIC TECHNOLOGY

A saint is a citizen whose memory has been made useful after death.

The Cultus Vitae rejects this definition in public and relies upon it in practice.

Canonization is never merely devotional. It establishes which forms of suffering may be admired, which failures may be forgiven, which deaths may be imitated, and which grievances may be redirected away from the institutions that caused them.

A badly chosen saint can found a rebellion.

A well chosen saint can make a generation carry bricks uphill while singing.

## IMOG AETER, FIRST CONTINUANT

Immog is revered within the Cultus Vitae as the first proof that humanity need not vanish merely because death has made its claim.

Official Cult doctrine avoids the details of the First Drop, the great apparatus, the golden coalescence, and the distributed horror that became the Draught. Instead it teaches the image of Immog standing again.

Standing is the sacrament.

Not victory. Not purity. Not mercy. Standing.

The poor understand this better than theologians.

## SAINT MERROW OF THE LAMP

Merrow is recorded as a hospice witness who remained awake through a plague procession lasting nine days and nine nights, recording the names of the dying when all local registers had burned.

Mortis auditors later confirmed that several of the names belonged to persons who had not yet died.

The Cult canonized Merrow before the audit could decide whether this was miracle, clerical exhaustion, or chronology contamination.

Her lamp is carried by deathwatch clergy. It is shielded because truth, like flame, must sometimes be cupped to remain useful.

## SAINT CALDREN OF THE EMPTY CUP

Caldren served during the Siege of Three Dry Spires, when water law collapsed and citizens began killing ration carriers for moisture in their blood.

He is said to have held an empty cup before a crowd of forty thousand and preached thirst into silence for six hours while relief pumps were repaired.

The cup never filled.

This is the point of the miracle.

The Last Cup order claims him as patron. Citizens in dry districts curse his name because saints of rationing are always more beloved by those who have already drunk.

---

## SAINT ODRAN OF THE RED BASIN

Odran was a battlefield surgeon-priest who continued operating after losing both hands, directing novices by voice until the ward floor became too wet to stand upon.

Later legends added replacement hands of silver, then burning iron, then living ash, then a choir of disembodied instruments. The official canon rejects all embellishments except the practical one: Odran kept speaking.

The Red Basin keeps his skull in a sealed reliquary and his surgical error ledger in a more secure one.

## MOTHER SENNA OF CHILDREN'S ASH

Senna gathered orphaned children after the Black Bell Famine and fed them with ration paste stretched through forbidden fungus stock.

Thousands survived.

Hundreds later developed luminous gum infections and prophetic fevers.

The Cult canonized Senna for the survival. The Collegia Mortis sealed the fever records. The Null Court still cites the case whenever mercy-based food adulteration returns to argument.

---

## ARCHCANTOR VALE MORHAIN

Vale Morhain is remembered for holding a spire population together during six months of lift failure, ration inversion, and corpse backlog.

His sermons are masterpieces of disciplined hope. His private letters suggest a man who hated everyone he saved.

The letters are not circulated. The sermons are.

This is the correct ordering of civilization.

---

## ABBOT-MEDICA IEL TARN

Iel Tarn founded the modern Vast Medica Wing triage liturgy and the three-column ledger system.

Publicly, Tarn is praised as the organizer of compassionate care.

Privately, every medical clergy knows Tarn's greater innovation was teaching doctors to decide who must not be saved without leaving the decision visible to the family.

Tarn is not a saint. Tarn is too useful for sainthood.

## THE FORBIDDEN SAINTS

The Cult also maintains a sealed list of persons who are never to be publicly venerated despite local devotion.

These include:

**Auren of the Open Door**, who taught that no breach should be closed until all voices within it had been heard.

**Vessa Warm-Hands**, who stole restoratives for lower-stack children and created three generations of anti-noble folk hymns.

**The Nameless Twelve of Kheprion**, whose sacrifice prevented a Cineric rupture but whose testimony contradicts approved Distillation doctrine.

**Brother Halvek the Gentle**, who preached that death should not be resisted when survival requires cruelty.

**Saint-Not-Saint Luria**, whose relics healed the poor too reliably and therefore destabilized hospice obedience.

Forbidden saints are not denied because they are false. They are denied because truth has never been sufficient defense against consequences.
