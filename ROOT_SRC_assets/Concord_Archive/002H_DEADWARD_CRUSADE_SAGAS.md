This record is not maintained for comfort. It is maintained because the dead remember poorly, the living remember conveniently, and the Concord survives only when enough officers know which kind of memory is trying to enter the room.
## I. ON THE WORD CRUSADE

The Concord uses the word crusade rarely and usually too late. A crusade is not merely a large campaign. It is a campaign in which material strategy, legal continuity, civic faith, Mortis science, Logistica supply, and Liminal navigation must all act under one wound. Crusades are declared when an incursion becomes a theater, when a theater becomes a history, and when history begins killing those who learn it incorrectly.

## II. THE ORUBAN CRUSADE

The Oruban Crusade began with frontier trench disappearances and ended with the burning of seven moons. Its enemy was not a single Cineric entity but a battlefield memory complex that learned from each engagement. Dead soldiers returned as tactical suggestions. Minefields rearranged themselves according to previous casualty maps. Retreat routes led to older battles. The Legio discovered that victory reports strengthened the enemy by clarifying what the living valued.

The final Oruban doctrine forbade written after-action reports until all dead were rendered and all banners inspected for Tarnishing. This nearly destroyed officer culture in the theater. It also prevented the enemy from reading the war through paperwork.

## III. THE GRAY BASILICA MARCH

The Gray Basilica appeared in the dead zone between three sister stations. It was seen first as a ruin, then as a cathedral, then as a fortress, then as a city moving one street per night toward inhabited territory. Every statue bore the face of someone missed by the observer. Every open door led to a room where the dead waited with plausible forgiveness.

Aeteric Guard units protected Immortality infrastructure while Legio Linea burned approach settlements to deny emotional supply. Mortis Auxilia carried mirrors face-down for thirty-one days. The Basilica was finally halted by stripping every name from the campaign maps and referring to the target only by coordinates. Deprived of recognition, it slowed enough to be buried under orbital glass.

## IV. THE CINDER ROAD WAR

The Cinder Road War was fought along an ash-route that began depositing invasion matter directly into convoy paths. Liminal choirs reported that the route had become hungry for movement. Ships arrived with extra corridors. Crews heard marching behind bulkheads. Cargo manifests included units never loaded. When opened, several containers contained soldiers from wars not yet fought, each begging for orders.

The Liminal Office proposed closure. The Logistica refused because six worlds depended on the route. The Legio kept it open under armed choir restraint for two years, fighting boarding actions against impossible infantry inside vessels that had not changed external dimensions. The war ended only when alternative ration lines were established and the route was starved of traffic long enough to forget the shape of convoys.

## V. THE SABLE REPATRIATION

The Sable Repatriation was named by politicians, not soldiers. It concerned the recovery of populations from worlds partially absorbed by ash shorelines. Millions had not died in ordinary terms. They continued existing in settlements visible from controlled thresholds, living years in impossible weather, raising children who cast no material shadow. Families demanded rescue. The Legio demanded clarity. The Collegia provided none.

Three expeditions recovered forty-two thousand bodies and perhaps twenty-seven thousand persons. The distinction broke several courts. Some rescued spoke with the voices of many. Some remembered future grandchildren. Some were alive enough to weep and dead enough to attract predators. The campaign ended when the Null Court declared further rescue an invasion vector.

## VI. THE BITTER LESSON OF CRUSADES

Every Deadward crusade begins with confidence that the enemy has shown itself. Every veteran learns that the enemy may be the need created by the war: the need for memory, rescue, honor, supply, proof, forgiveness, a road home. The Cinerum wins when the living cannot distinguish strategy from longing.

The Concord therefore teaches crusade officers to mistrust noble motives, sacred motives, efficient motives, and merciful motives in equal measure. This has made them unpleasant. It has also kept several sectors alive.
