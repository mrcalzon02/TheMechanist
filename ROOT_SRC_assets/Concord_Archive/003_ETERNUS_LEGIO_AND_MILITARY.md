# 003 ETERNUS LEGIO AND MILITARY
## Restricted Concordial Military Archive Record

## ARCHIVAL SEAL AND ADMISSIBILITY

Cineric Concord Central Military Archive, sealed under Continuity Authority and cross-noted by
the Collegia Mortis, the Concordia Logistica, and the Office of Ash-Sea Transit. This record is
issued for command instruction, civic indoctrination of bonded officers, and correction of
lower-civic rumor concerning the Eternus Legio.

The contents herein are not devotional. The Legio accepts devotion as fuel, discipline, and
battlefield adhesive, yet this record concerns the institution as a mechanism of survival. The
Legio is a sword, a court, a road-builder, a siege engine, a funeral procession, and a
bureaucracy wearing armor. It is also the oldest surviving answer to the question of what the
living may do when the dead refuse to remain dead.

No district authority, noble ledger, Logistica factor, Mortis registrar, or minor ecclesiastical
officer may abridge this record without counterseal from three superior offices. Unlicensed
abridgment has historically produced disastrous civic fantasies: that the Legio is merely an
army, that its officers are mere nobles with weapons, or that its campaigns are wars in the
common sense. The Legio does not simply wage war. It enforces the right of material continuity
against hunger given shape.


## PRIMARY DEFINITION OF THE ETERNUS LEGIO

The Eternus Legio is the armed continuity of the Concord. It predates the present Concordial
statute-chain, predates the current Logistica measurebooks, and predates most of the noble
houses now claiming to have always served it. Its banners were raised during the early
distillation struggles, when Immog Aeter and the first death-bound soldiers learned that the
Cinerum possessed predatory sovereignties that could reach into mortal history through grief,
hunger, oath, inheritance, and unfinished violence.

To call the Legio an army is an error tolerated only among the unlettered. An army campaigns,
occupies, consumes supply, and then withdraws or dies. The Legio colonizes catastrophe. It
enters breached worlds, haunted ruins, dead biospheres, mutiny sectors, Cineric peripheries, and
post-civilizational ashlands, then imposes measure upon them until they can be taxed, mapped,
guarded, fed, buried, and used.

The Legio contains infantry, fleets, artillery, engineers, memorial hosts, corpse-processing
guards, shipborne cohorts, void breach wardens, reliquary escorts, commissarial executioners,
bonded medical houses, and immortal officers whose personal recollections are often older than
the civic maps they currently defend. It is not one blade. It is the armory in which
civilization stores every blade that it is too frightened to leave in civilian hands.

The First Military Article remains engraved above the inner gate of every proper Legio fortress:
Life yields no jurisdiction to the dead. The Second Article follows beneath it: Continuity
requires force. The Third Article is not engraved, because every soldier learns it from ration
hunger, ash-stink, and the first corpse that calls them by name: Force requires obedience.


## THE IMMOGIC FOUNDATION

All sanctioned accounts begin with Immog Aeter, though none agree cleanly upon the man before
the Draught took him. Some lineages remember him as a Legio commander of uncommon stature. Some
Mortis catechisms call him the First Distiller only after the battle that remade him. Certain
noble registers attempt to place him inside extinct pre-Concord houses, a claim the Central
Archive regards with contempt. He was claimed after victory by those who feared that victory
might grant authority to someone outside their custody.

The Legio remembers Immog not as a saint without sin, but as the first soldier who returned from
death with enough will intact to make a legal claim against oblivion. The Muldunine Accord
records his later condemnation of many decisions made during the Continuity Wars, yet even
hostile accounts admit that his rise changed the meaning of command. Before Immog, a commander
spent men and died. After Immog, a commander might die, stand again, and carry a memory of
failure into the next assault.

The early Legio became inseparable from necrological distillation because its enemies made
ordinary mortality strategically impossible. Dead gods returned through cult residue. Ash-beasts
learned the names of officers and used those names as hooks. Cineric sovereigns devoured
battlefields after the killing ended. To fight once was not enough. A soldiering order that
could be erased by a single generation of attrition could never hold the line against entities
grown fat on every extinction before mankind.

The Draught therefore entered military doctrine as sacrament, ration, poison, chain, and proof.
No officer of serious standing can discuss the Legio without discussing the economy of return:
who may be restored, who may be preserved, who may be allowed to remember, who is burned clean,
and who is left for the furnaces because continuity cannot afford them.


## DOCTRINAL ARTICLES

The Legio's Articles are recited in many forms across the Concord, but the following military
form remains most widely attested in muster halls and ashward academies.

- Article I: The material world is the lawful province of the living, and no dead sovereignty
holds legitimate claim upon it.
- Article II: The Cinerum is not enemy territory in the ordinary sense. It is a hostile continuum
in which memory, hunger, oath, and identity may act as terrain.
- Article III: Immortality is not reward. It is burden, hazard, strategic utility, and accumulated
debt.
- Article IV: A soldier restored by Draught owes not gratitude but service, because restoration
without service becomes aristocratic rot.
- Article V: A world abandoned to the dead is a fortress granted to the enemy. Reclamation is
therefore an obligation, not an ambition.
- Article VI: The Legio commands violence, but violence without registration becomes barbarism.
Every burning, every purge, every redoubt, every breach-seal, and every condemned population
must be entered into continuity record.
- Article VII: The common citizen is not expendable by nature. The common citizen is expendable
only when the accounting of survival admits no alternate expenditure. This distinction has
caused more executions inside the officer caste than outside it.
- Article VIII: No immortal commander may claim private ownership of a campaign, a world, a
regiment, a soul-harvest, or a dead enemy rendered into distillate.
- Article IX: The Legio has no right to govern forever where governance can safely pass to civil
authority. No Legate enjoys this article. All obey it in public.
- Article X: If the Legio itself becomes an instrument of the dead, all subordinate offices are
released into corrective violence under sealed Noctis doctrine.


## COMMAND STRATA

The command strata of the Eternus Legio are often mistaken for a simple hierarchy. In practice
they are a stack of age, oath, medical stability, tactical jurisdiction, ceremonial precedence,
and Draught entitlement. A young commander of clear mind may outrank a decaying immortal whose
memory is legally fragmented, yet the latter may possess battle knowledge unavailable anywhere
else in the Concord. Such disputes are settled through seal precedence, Mortis cognitive
examination, and, in darkly traditional formations, formal challenge under bloodless weapons
that rarely remain bloodless.

At the summit stand the Aeternal Legates, not all of whom remain fit for field command. Some are
living repositories of campaigns too ancient for clean archive; others are theater lords who can
remember the names of planets that no longer have atmospheres. Beneath them serve Marshals of
Continuance, Siege-Consuls, Ashward Praefects, Fleet-Legates, and Cohort Masters, each bound by
different chains of report to Logistica, Mortis, Null Court, and noble command.

Lower formations retain more ordinary titles because ordinary soldiers require words that can be
shouted while dying. Cohorts, maniples, lances, watches, pyre-teams, breach files, and ash
squads exist in endless local variation. A Turris garrison may call its armored elevator troops
Stackwardens, while an ashland reclamation host calls a similar force Blackstep Infantry. The
Central Archive tolerates such variation so long as supply codes remain legible.

The most dangerous command ambiguity arises when military necessity intersects with civil
jurisdiction. A Legio officer can order a district sealed against breach, a ration line diverted
to a siege, or a lower stack burned after confirmed Cineric inversion. Yet the Null Court may
later rule the order lawful, unlawful, void, or retroactively compulsory. Many famous Legates
survived centuries of battle only to be destroyed by paperwork written by men who never smelled
ash.


## FURNACE INFANTRY

Furnace Infantry are recorded as line infantry drawn from bonded citizens, tithed families,
penal survivors, and hereditary barrack clans. Their public description is modest because
military institutions survive by turning horror into job titles. Their real function is to hold
corridor lines, manufactorum approaches, ash-gates, ration squares, and breach-lit streets until
heavier arms arrive, and to do so under conditions in which ordinary law has usually become too
slow to breathe.

Training for Furnace Infantry begins with obedience, but obedience alone is insufficient. The
soldier is taught the local geography of fear: how a breach smells before bells ring, why a
corpse that asks for its mother is not to be answered, which walls burn green when ash pressure
rises, and why a familiar voice heard from behind a sealed bulkhead is a weapon until proven
otherwise.

The Logistica assigns Furnace Infantry supply codes that appear sterile in ledger form. In
practice those codes determine whether a district receives ammunition or bread, whether a
casualty receives restoration ampules or ordinary burial, and whether a commander can sustain a
defense long enough for the Mortis furnaces to prevent the dead from becoming reinforcements for
the enemy.

Within soldiers' cant, Furnace Infantry possess their own taboos. They paint small marks on
helmets, recite abbreviated furnace prayers, trade forbidden jokes about immortal officers, and
learn which orders never arrive in writing. The Archive does not endorse these habits. The
Archive records that formations stripped of all such habits break faster.

## RECRUITMENT AND HUMAN MATERIAL

The Legio recruits from the Concord in layers. Some enter through hereditary barrack-right, born
in fortresses where lullabies name battles instead of stars. Some arrive through planetary
tithe, district lottery, noble obligation, penal commutation, orphan wardship, or emergency
draft after breach. A smaller number volunteer, which military physicians treat as a diagnostic
sign requiring investigation but not rejection.

Common soldiers are not told that they are immortal. They are told that continuity honors
service. Only those who survive long enough to be useful to memory enter serious restoration
consideration. The Draught is too rare, too politically contested, and too dangerous to
distribute according to sentiment. A beloved private may be burned clean while a despised
cartographer is restored because the latter has walked a passage through ash that no map
retains.

The Legio's brutality toward its own recruits is not accidental, though many officers hide
private cruelty inside necessary discipline. Training breaks the illusion that life is protected
by fairness. It replaces the illusion with procedure, formation, drill, ration obedience, breach
conduct, corpse conduct, and hatred of waste. A soldier who panics may doom a squad. A squad
that breaks may doom a gate. A gate that falls may feed a Cineric prince with a million names.

Noble children sent to the Legio are treated differently but not always more gently. They arrive
with tutors, servants, body privileges, family seals, and the intolerable confidence of those
who have been praised for blood they did not spill. The Legio permits such arrogance only until
the first casualty bell. Afterward, the noble either becomes useful or becomes a burden with
expensive boots.


## DRAUGHT, RETURN, AND THE ECONOMY OF RESTORATION

No subject corrodes Legio unity more reliably than restoration. The common soldier sees
immortality above them and burial beneath them. The officer sees necessary allocation. The noble
sees inheritance. The Cultus Vitae sees sacrament and temptation. The Collegia Mortis sees
process, risk, and contamination. The Concordia Logistica sees a scarce material moving through
too many hands while everyone lies about need.

Legio restoration law divides casualties into categories of tactical memory, body viability,
soul adhesion, command value, contamination, and political consequence. A clean corpse with high
strategic recollection may receive expensive intervention. A living soldier whose mind has
opened too far toward the Ash Sea may be denied restoration, sedated, interrogated, and rendered
before the damage spreads.

The famous saying that the Legio wastes no death is misunderstood. The Legio wastes deaths
constantly. It wastes them in failed assaults, erroneous maps, noble vanity, ration shortages,
bad weather, corrupt officers, and ancient machines that refuse to operate unless fed more
bodies than any honest engineer would admit. The doctrine means only that every death is entered
into an economy afterward: memory, material, warning, ash-index, or accusation.

Many immortal officers fear ordinary burial more than battle. They have died too often to trust
death as a boundary. Others secretly hunger for finality but cannot obtain permission to cease
while their memories remain valuable. The Legio calls this sacrifice. The Mortis physicians call
it continuity strain. The lower ranks call it the rich man's cage.


## RELATION TO THE CONCORDIA LOGISTICA

The Legio wins no campaign without the Logistica, and therefore resents the Logistica with the
intimacy of dependence. Every shell, ration, stretcher, boot, ash-filter, corpse tag, medicae
phial, burial chip, and transit allocation passes through numbering offices that many soldiers
regard as parasites until the day the numbers fail and the regiment starves.

Logistica officers attached to Legio formations possess authority that seems absurd to warriors
and entirely rational to anyone who has watched a siege collapse from overconsumption. A
Quartermaster of Continuance may deny ammunition to a glorious assault in order to preserve
furnace fuel for the civilian dead. A water factor may countermand a cavalry redeployment if the
redeployment endangers lower-stack reserves. Such officers are hated, threatened, murdered,
decorated, and replaced.

The oldest military joke claims that the Legio owns the guns, the Cult owns the prayers, the
Mortis owns the corpses, and the Logistica owns the hour at which all of them discover whether
they may continue. The joke survives because it is accurate.


## RELATION TO THE COLLEGIA MORTIS

The Collegia Mortis are the Legio's physicians, undertakers, auditors, poisoners, memory
examiners, corpse-law experts, and worst dinner companions. No long campaign can operate without
them. No soldier sleeps well after seeing their tents lit from within by blue furnace glass.

Mortis authority enters military life at enlistment, triage, execution, restoration, autopsy,
and ash-indexing. They decide whether a corpse is safe to bury, burn, distill, dissect,
interrogate, or erase. They examine immortal officers for continuity strain and sometimes
declare a beloved commander legally dead while the commander remains upright and enraged.

The Legio accuses Mortis of cowardice because Mortis refuses many glorious risks. Mortis accuses
the Legio of sentimentality because soldiers keep trying to rescue bodies that have already
become doors. Both accusations are true often enough to preserve the quarrel.


## RELATION TO CIVIL AUTHORITY

In conquered districts, the Legio governs first by cordon, then by ration, then by register.
Civil courts enter only after the streets stop changing shape. The Null Court recognizes this
necessity while reserving the right to punish every misuse of it decades later. This arrangement
produces impressive obedience in young officers and creative record destruction in old ones.

The Custodia Veritatis may investigate Legio abuses, yet even the Custodia move carefully around
ancient regiments. A regiment that has held a breach line for two centuries develops civic
sanctity no law admits and every official fears. When a Custodian enters such a barracks alone,
both offices pretend this is routine. It is never routine.

The Cohors Noctis exist as the nightmare beneath Legio pride. Their name is rarely written in
operational orders. Their arrival means that suspected treason, Cineric compromise, dynastic
corruption, forbidden machine traffic, or immortal insubordination has exceeded tolerable rumor.
A common soldier fears the enemy. A Legate fears the room going quiet before a Noctis officer
removes their gloves.


## ENEMY TAXONOMY USED IN FIELD ORDERS

Field orders reduce terror to categories because a soldier requires action before comprehension.
The following classifications are incomplete by necessity. Full taxonomic study belongs to
Mortis, Ferrum, and forbidden archives. On the battlefield, a name is useful only if it tells
the hand what to do next.

Cineric Beasts: assessed as appetite given muscle by breach pressure; preferred Legio answer is
burning, dismemberment, and rapid Mortis rendering.

Dead Sovereignties: assessed as old rulers of the Ash Sea wearing titles stolen from extinct
worship; preferred Legio answer is choir suppression, artillery denial, and name quarantine.

Unburdened Cults: assessed as living collaborators who believe flesh is an obstacle and the dead
are liberators; preferred Legio answer is capture for interrogation when possible, furnace
afterward when necessary.

Revenant Hosts: assessed as battlefield dead reanimated by grief echo, oath residue, or hostile
memory; preferred Legio answer is silence, decapitation, identity erasure, and controlled pyre.

Conceptual Predators: assessed as ideas that learned to hunt through symbol, law, hunger, or
devotion; preferred Legio answer is archive quarantine, ritual contradiction, and physical
isolation of infected populations.

Ash-Warped Nobility: assessed as ruling lines whose Draught hunger has opened dynastic
vulnerability; preferred Legio answer is Custodia review, Mortis audit, or Noctis correction.

Digital Remnants: assessed as surviving Heliun or post-Heliun machine minds hidden inside
infrastructure; preferred Legio answer is Ferrum sanction, hard severance, material destruction,
and officer-level secrecy.


## INCIDENT EXTRACT: THE VEY FOUNDATION WAR

Archive designation: The Vey Foundation War. Operational environment: a Turris world still
settling into its own footprint. Principal military lesson: lower strata became breach-rich
after foundation piles pierced a mass grave of prehuman cognition.

The incident remains preserved in officer schooling because it demonstrates the Legio's central
burden: every victory occurs inside a web of courts, furnaces, ration ledgers, noble claims,
machine dependencies, and grieving populations. The commander who sees only the enemy loses the
city behind them. The commander who sees only the city feeds the enemy by hesitation.

Surviving testimonies agree upon very little beyond the sound of bells, the failure of at least
one trusted map, and the presence of orders later declared both necessary and improper. This is
typical. The Concord rarely grants clean wars to those required to fight them.

## SOLDIER CULTURE AND THE PRIVATE LANGUAGE OF SURVIVAL

The Legio officially discourages superstition outside sanctioned Cultus rites. The Legio is also
old enough to know that men facing the dead will manufacture superstition from boot nails if
nothing else is provided. Thus the barracks teem with small unauthorized protections: ash knots
tied inside sleeves, teeth carried from first kills, ration spoons bent into ward signs, names
written backward on helmet rims, and letters to dead comrades burned in furnace vents.

Common soldiers call Cineric pressure the leaning dark. They call restoration candidates glass
men. They call immortal officers old candles because they keep burning after they ought to have
guttered out. They call Mortis surgeons black aprons even when the aprons are white. They call
Logistica clerks ration saints on payday and ink leeches on every other day.

Such language alarms doctrinal purists and comforts veterans. A soldier who can joke about
horror has not yet surrendered speech to it. The Cultus Vitae therefore condemns vulgar barrack
blasphemy in sermons and quietly copies the useful phrases into lower-civic prayerbooks once
enough widows start using them.


## FAILURE MODES

The Eternus Legio can fail through defeat, corruption, exhaustion, triumph, and immortality.
Defeat is simplest. The enemy breaks the line and the living die. Corruption is subtler: a
regiment begins answering whispers, a commander preserves a breached district for private
harvest, a noble cohort shields a family cult, or a shipboard unit treats ash voices as
ancestral guidance.

Exhaustion kills slowly. A formation kept too long at the front becomes obedient but hollow,
able to follow orders yet unable to understand why orders matter. The Collegia Mortis marks such
units for rotation; the Logistica denies rotation when supply routes are worse elsewhere;
commanders falsify readiness; the Cultus blesses them; the enemy waits.

Triumph corrupts through myth. A regiment that saves a world may begin to believe the world owes
it moral exemption. An immortal officer who returns from a dozen deaths may begin to regard law
as a younger creature. A campaign that succeeds through atrocity may teach the wrong students
the wrong lesson. The Cohors Noctis exists because the Legio's victories sometimes require
execution afterward.

Immortality is the final failure mode. The restored commander carries too many wars, too many
betrayals, too many dead subordinates, too many versions of doctrine, and too many bodies
remembered from the inside. At a certain point the officer may remain brilliant while becoming
impossible to govern. Concordial law names this continuity overburden. Soldiers name it going
old.

## REGIMENTAL EXTRACT: THE ASHEN TENTH

The Ashen Tenth trace their accepted home register to Turris-Vey. Their formation memory centers
upon a breach that wore the faces of their officers, an event recited to recruits before they
are permitted to carry live ammunition. The recitation varies by barracks, yet the central
lesson remains fixed: the enemy does not merely kill soldiers, it attempts to alter the meaning
of their obedience.

The regiment's formal duty is corridor defense and furnace-road counterattack. In practice this
duty expands to every ugly task neighboring formations prefer to call specialized. They hold
doors while others retreat, escort clerks whose ledgers decide who eats, secure furnace
corridors, count civilians through smoke, and remind young officers that a map is not a promise
made by reality.

Their private rite is helmet reliquaries filled with ash from the first gate they lost. Command
tolerates the practice because abolishing it produced discipline reports, slower musters, and
three incidents of troops entering action without speaking. Soldiers are not machines, even when
generals envy machines. A rite that steadies the hand is cheaper than a replacement cohort.

Their most durable institutional tension is with Logistica quartermasters who never deliver
enough filter cloth. The quarrel is old enough to have forms, insults, exchange gifts, and at
least one shared drinking song forbidden by both offices. The regiment's barrack saying is: If
the wall speaks, answer with fire. Like most soldier sayings, it is truer than doctrine and less
useful in court.

Casualty behavior among The Ashen Tenth shows above-average retrieval aggression and below-
average respect for withdrawal bells. Mortis observers attribute this to unresolved formation
guilt. Veterans call it loyalty. Logistica factors call it a transport burden. The Archive
records that all three assessments have coincided on the same day.

## CAMPAIGN MEMORANDUM: THE RED CHOIR SUPPRESSION

The Red Choir Suppression occurred in a mourning district whose hymns began changing artillery
trajectories. The approved lesson states that Ashward officers learned that sound could carry
obedience away from the living and toward the dead. The unapproved lesson, passed through
barracks and field kitchens, is that the Concord regularly asks soldiers to repair mistakes made
by institutions far above their pay, then punishes them for noticing the height.

Operational records show the same recurring pattern: initial confidence, map failure,
contradictory civil claims, ration strain, Mortis objection, local rumor, enemy adaptation, and
a final order written by someone who knew history would hate it. This pattern is not evidence of
incompetence alone. It is evidence that war inside the Concord never takes place on a
battlefield only. It takes place inside class, machine dependence, grief, law, hunger, and the
Ash Sea's talent for using all of them.

The memorandum is copied to officer schools with names of the dead intact. Removing names
improves morale and worsens judgment. An officer who studies abstractions becomes willing to
spend abstractions. The Legio requires commanders capable of spending lives while still knowing
they are lives.

Subsequent revisions added Logistica timing tables, Mortis contamination notes, civil inquiry

## FINAL ARCHIVAL FORMULA

The Eternus Legio is terrible because the universe made gentler instruments insufficient. It is
also terrible because every institution built for necessary horror eventually discovers
pleasures within necessity. The Concord survives by refusing to forget either truth.

When the lower citizen sees a parade, they are permitted pride. When the noble sees a Legate,
they are permitted calculation. When the Cult sees a banner, it is permitted song. When the
Mortis physician sees a casualty list, they are permitted silence. When the Logistica factor
sees a campaign order, they are permitted fear. All responses are correct.

The Legio holds the line between the living and what waits beyond death. It has held that line
with courage, cruelty, hunger, ignorance, brilliance, and blood. The Archive records its honor.
The Archive records its crimes. Continuity requires both.
