# 005E FLEET PROVISIONING SYNOD
## Concordial Logistica Archive Record Concerning Vessel Loading, Ash-Sea Transit Stores, War Convoys, Pilgrim Capacity, Ammunition Mass, Liminal Requirements, and the Ancient Error of Believing a Ship Is Ready Because It Is Large

Filed beneath **005 Concordia Logistica**, in mandatory coordination with the Liminal Office, Eternus Legio naval desks, Mortis supply factors, Fleet yards of the Undying Lords, water and ration authorities, and all captains who must be reminded that courage has mass and must be loaded before departure.

This record concerns the **Fleet Provisioning Synod**.

A vessel does not sail on honor.

It sails on counted things.

---

## PRIMARY DEFINITION

The Fleet Provisioning Synod calculates, authorizes, loads, seals, audits, and mourns the material requirements of Concordial vessels traveling between worlds, through Ash-Sea routes, into war zones, across famine corridors, or away from places everyone in authority has agreed not to describe as abandoned until the ship is gone.

The Synod is called a synod because its internal disputes resemble religion and because early fleet factors discovered that captains obeyed cargo priests more readily than cargo clerks. The title remained after the clerks regained practical control.

It provisions hulls for troop movement, noble evacuation, industrial relocation, pilgrimage, corpse return, distillate security, breach response, colony reinforcement, and doomed gestures undertaken because a Concordial officer used the phrase unacceptable loss in the presence of witnesses.

The Synod's first law reads:

*No mission is under-supplied until it survives long enough for the shortage to become visible.*

---

## MASS, VOLUME, AND LIES

Every captain lies about available space. Every Legio commander lies about ammunition needs. Every noble passenger lies about baggage. Every priest lies about candle weight. Every Mortis factor understates coffin volume. Every Liminal choir understates heat requirements until the first navigator begins weeping black.

The Synod exists to hear these lies in chorus and turn them into a loading plan.

Fleet provisioning is constrained by mass, volume, balance, access, hazard isolation, spoilage, heat, pressure, ash exposure, crew sanity, Liminal requirement, emergency jettison order, and the probability that the destination will no longer possess docks, gravity, law, or survivors.

A crate loaded behind the wrong bulkhead may as well have been left behind. A fuel reserve placed beyond a breach door becomes a prayer. Ammunition buried under ceremonial furniture becomes evidence in a court that will not help the dead.

---

## ASH-SEA REQUIREMENTS

Vessels entering the Ash-Sea require ordinary stores and impossible stores. Ration, water, fuel, repair stock, ammunition, medical packs, furnace kits, pressure skins, coolant, and replacement valves are expected. Less understood are Liminal heat stores, choir sedation, black-tear collection cloth, anti-breach salts, navigator privacy screens, conceptual contamination markers, false-star charts, thunder-pattern recording plates, ash-static grounding chains, and sealed apology packets for captains whose route no longer exists.

The Liminal Office insists that certain cargo arrangements make transit safer. The Synod does not understand all such requirements, but it has learned not to place frozen meat beside heat-starved choir cells unless it wishes to explain claw marks in both.

Ships carrying powerful Liminals require warmth budgets as serious as fuel budgets. A cold choir can become despair, breach, violence, or navigation failure. A warm choir can still become those things, but later.

The Synod respects later.

---

## WAR LOADING

War loading differs from transit loading because everything necessary is heavy and everything heroic is inefficient. Armored units require deck reinforcement. Ammunition requires hazard separation. Field kitchens require fuel. Surgeons require water. Coffin stacks require space commanders had reserved for glory. Spare boots are more important than banners and therefore receive less attention until the second week.

The Synod's war factors are authorized to override aesthetic objections to cargo order. They may strip noble observation galleries, temple procession aisles, officer wine stores, and decorative shrine bulkheads if doing so permits ammunition, water, or medicae clearance.

Captains protest.

The Synod files the protest under ballast.

---

## THE SYNOD AS BUREAUCRATIC CORPSE

Fleet provisioning inherited old pre-Heliun automation, newer distrust of automation, ancient maritime language, ash-navigation superstition, Legio emergency tables, noble travel privileges, and the ruinous habit of never discarding a voyage form because somewhere, someday, a ship may again need to carry exactly seven thousand pilgrims, three siege engines, two embalmed saints, a contaminated governor, and an angry Liminal choir through a route last seen during the Continuity Wars.

The Synod keeps forms for extinct hull classes, banned cognition-assisted load balancers, cargos whose names are sealed, and ration plans for crews with metabolic profiles no longer found among living humans. Some forms are ridiculous. Some have saved ships.

No one knows in advance which is which.

This is how the corpse grows.

---

## LOST SHIP ACCOUNTING

A ship lost in the Ash-Sea does not leave only grief. It leaves accounting. Cargo must be struck, debt reassigned, noble claims suspended, ammunition deducted, bodies presumed or not presumed, insurance prayers validated, Liminal lineages reviewed, and replacement requests denied until the loss is confirmed by evidence impossible to obtain.

The Synod maintains entire halls of unresolved vessels. Their ledgers remain open because a vessel may return after years, decades, or a time interval that embarrasses clocks. Supplies assigned to them cannot be fully erased. Families cannot be fully compensated. Captains cannot be fully blamed. The empire cannot fully mourn what may yet dock screaming.

Thus the Synod is not merely a provisioning body.

It is an archive of departures civilization has not finished paying for.

---

## CLOSING INSTRUCTION

Before a vessel leaves, ask not whether its mission is noble.

Ask whether water can be reached after the second bulkhead breach. Ask whether the choir can remain warm. Ask whether ammunition will crush the medical deck during hard turn. Ask whether the noble passengers can be prevented from eating siege chocolate. Ask whether the dead have somewhere to go.

Then load the ship.

Then pray the numbers were heavier than fate.
