## I. Prefatory Notice: Founder History Is Not Safe History

This record concerns Immog Aeter.

It does not settle him.

No authorized archive settles him. Those that appear to do so are devotional instruments, political weapons, teaching simplifications, noble inheritance scaffolds, or deliberate Custodia stabilizations written during years in which the public needed a shape around which to close its terrified hands. The closer one comes to the first centuries of distillation, the fewer firm facts remain and the more violently the surviving facts oppose each other. Accounts written by witnesses contradict accounts written by those who claimed to have commanded witnesses. Accounts written by Immog’s enemies preserve details later heroic texts omit. Accounts written by his followers contain administrative data too precise to be pure worship and miracles too convenient to be comfortable evidence.

The sanctioned position of the Concord is therefore not that Immog’s history is unknown. It is that Immog’s history remains classified by instability rather than ignorance.

A false founder may govern for centuries if the lie is useful. A true founder may destroy a civilization if his truth is released at the wrong hour. The difference matters less to a starving district than the functioning of its ration pipes, less to a ship in the Ashen Sea than the continued discipline of its Liminal Choir, less to a breach-line than the presence of soldiers who still believe that death can be made to serve. This is why the Concord teaches children Immog as a lantern and teaches officers Immog as an unresolved wound.

The man called Immog began the era of the Great Distillation. He initiated, benefited from, or survived the process by which Cineric entities were rendered into stabilizable life-substrate. He became the first publicly recognized immortal of the surviving human order. He rose through the fractured military-civil structures that preceded the Eternus Legio. He challenged the old rulers later grouped under the Sarantaran administrations. He created, licensed, or forced into being the early distillation regimes that became the Draught economy. He began the Continuity Wars, though no faction agrees whether he began them by rebellion, necessary correction, usurpation, or rescue.

Everything beyond those points is contested.

Everything beyond those points is also still used.

The Legio invokes Immog when ordering soldiers to hold breach-lines until their lungs blacken. The Concordia invokes Immog when turning emergency necessity into permanent procedure. The Collegia Mortis invokes Immog when justifying devices that make useful material from things that once screamed. The Cultus Vitae invokes Immog when telling the poor that lawful suffering may preserve the living. The noble houses invoke Immog when pouring fractional Draught into their private veins. The Undying Lords invoke Immog when claiming that knowledge must survive flesh. The Didam curse Immog as the thief who stole the feast from the true eaters. Nezram’s mourners call him the Deaf King because, in their doctrine, he heard the pain of the dead and learned only how to process it.

The Unentered call him simpler things.

Most of those things are accurate in part.

## II. On the Name Aeter and the Problem of Titles That Become People

The earliest recoverable forms of the name are inconsistent. **Immog**, **Imog**, **Ymogh**, **Em-Mog**, and the battlefield vocative **Mog of the Line** appear in fractured rolls recovered from pre-Concordial ash-front musters. The epithet **Aeter** appears later, initially not as surname but as status designation, a contraction used in early Legio ledgers for a combatant observed to return after mortal defeat without ordinary necrological loss. In the first preserved red-ink resurrection register, the notation reads:

**IM-MOG / AETER / NOT TO BE BURNT / NOT TO BE PRONOUNCED DEAD / RESTRAINT NOT ADVISED WITHOUT ENGINE COMPANY SUPPORT.**

From this one line entire temples have been built.

The Cultus Vitae maintains that the term became sacred because the living recognized in him a lawful defiance of the False Beyond. The Collegia Mortis maintains that it was an early technical classification describing a stabilized host after involuntary saturation by Cineric Vitae. The Concordia Logistica maintains that it was an administrative correction issued to prevent repeated inheritance transfers after each battlefield death. The Eternus Legio maintains that it was a rank earned by function before it was ever sanctified by language.

The Custodia Veritatis notes that all four explanations can be true without improving safety.

There are even heretical schools that insist Immog Aeter was never one man at all, but a rotating title granted to successive survivors of distillation saturation whose memories were selectively harmonized by Collegia Mortis mnemonic surgery. These schools are suppressed not because they have been disproved, but because proof would not alter their destructive utility. A soldier who believes the First Legate may be inherited like a cloak does not hold quite the same way in the cold. A noble who believes the Founder was a manufacturable sequence begins funding laboratories too confidently. A machine-lord who believes personhood can be overwritten by continuity begins smiling with circuits where lips should have remained.

The archive therefore preserves the title as name, the name as title, and the uncertainty as a sealed component of civic survival.

Immog Aeter is the man.

Immog Aeter is the myth.

Immog Aeter is the unresolved legal precedent by which the Concord continues to claim that immortality can serve life rather than consume it.

## III. The Muldunine Accord

Among all surviving founder texts, none is more revered, disputed, weaponized, banned, copied, forged, annotated, burnt, recited, and litigated than the **Muldunine Accord**.

The document claims to be a biography written in Immog’s own hand thousands of years after the First Distillation. Its title appears to derive from **Muldunine**, a dead fortress-world, tribunal city, battlefield archive, or ash-monk copyhouse depending upon which manuscript lineage is being read. The official Concordial position is that the Accord is neither accepted as authentic nor dismissed as fabrication. It is classified as **Founder-Adjacent Testimony of Unstable but Irreplaceable Value**. This classification was created specifically for the Accord after three benches of the Null Court failed to agree on whether suppressing it strengthened continuity or weakened it by driving noble houses to trade in worse copies.

The Accord is combative by design. It contradicts the **Varric Witness Rolls**, the **Sarantarene Testimony of Lawful Succession**, the **Seven Ashen Homilies of the Grave-Defier**, the **Iron Chronicle of the Proto-Continuants**, the **Lament of the Bait Host**, the **Didamic Curse of the Stolen Feast**, and at least twenty lesser biographies whose authors claim either direct witness, dream-contact, mnemonic inheritance, or access to records that were conveniently destroyed shortly after citation.

The Muldunine Accord condemns many decisions attributed to Immog. It speaks of victories as errors paid for by children not yet born. It calls the First Distillation both necessary and unforgivable. It admits that the Draught economy began as an emergency weapon and became a civilization. It describes the Continuity Wars not as a clean liberation but as a series of bargains in which every faction learned to feed something it had sworn to control. It is the only major text that presents Immog neither as saint nor tyrant, but as a man who survived an impossible event and then discovered that survival had made him too useful to be allowed innocence.

This is precisely why it is dangerous.

Devotional histories comfort. Enemy histories accuse. Administrative histories reduce. The Muldunine Accord wounds. It gives Immog regret without removing his authority, responsibility without simplifying his motives, and horror without absolving those who inherited his systems. It is therefore beloved by reformist Legates, despised by high noble Draught houses, studied in secret by Collegia Mortis distillers, quoted selectively by Cultus Vitae confessors, and treated by the Custodia as a document with breach-adjacent memetic behavior.

No complete public copy is permitted.

Incomplete copies are permitted when their omissions are properly indexed.

Improper omissions are treason.

Complete copies are worse.

## IV. The Era Before the First Distillation

There was no Concord when Immog rose.

There were human worlds, fortress polities, noble compacts, legions, industrial synods, old machine houses, military-civil councils, ash-front treaties, and hundreds of emergency regimes pretending to be eternal because admitting temporary necessity would have caused collapse. The Cinerum pressed against reality with irregular violence. Breach zones opened and sealed unpredictably. Dead things returned wearing half-remembered shapes. Concepts hunted. Choirs failed. Worlds were mapped, lost, reclaimed, renamed, and lost again.

The Proto-Undying Lords were not yet the machine aristocracy later policed by the Ferrum Sanctum. They were continuity engineers, industrial necrologists, weapons magnates, surgical preservationists, archive-priests, manufactorum dynasts, and data-house regents whose private facilities remembered more science than most governments could afford to retain. They served whoever could protect their foundries and supply them with labor, metals, fuel, bodies, and legal shadows. Many believed flesh could be improved by cutting it away. Many believed memory was the only sacred substance. Many believed the Cinerum was not merely an enemy realm but a reservoir of extractable law.

They were not wrong.

That is the first terror of the age.

The military orders of the period depended upon them. Ships required their engines. Fortresses required their pumps. Weapons required their tolerances. Medical corps required their cold storage, their corpse-handling instruments, their prohibited grafts, their knowledge of how death lingered in matter. The old Legio formations—if they may be called Legio before Immog gave the word its current weight—were already chained to industrial houses that could not be trusted and could not be abandoned.

Immog appears in this era as a soldier of unusual stature, discipline, and operational success. Some sources call him noble-born. Others call him a penal officer promoted from ash-front survival. A few declare him a vat-child designed by the Proto-Undying Lords as a bait organism, though these accounts are generally favored by those who wish to own him. The Muldunine Accord says only that he was “made by war before any machine laid claim to my making.”

The Cinerum had in those days produced, or revealed, entities later called High Lords, Cine-Lords, or Bestiae-Regnant: vast predatory concentrations of death-residue, memory-hunger, and conceptual violence capable of destroying worlds not merely by force but by making the idea of resistance decay in the minds of defenders. One such being became the central enemy of the First Distillation account. Its name is not preserved in sanctioned form. The Concordia records it as **CINERIC REGNANT INDEX I — NAME REMOVED FOR POSTHUMOUS NON-REVERENCE**. The Didam call it a robbed ancestor. The Cultus Vitae calls it the Beast That Would Have Eaten the Line. Soldiers call it, in older marching curses, the Lord That Burned Backward.

Whatever it was, it was powerful enough to justify a final device, a bait host, and a plan from which no one expected Immog to return.

## V. The Trap at the Far Corner

Every major account agrees that the First Distillation began as a battle rather than a ceremony.

The location is disputed. The Varric Rolls place it in the ashward orbit of a dead moon. The Iron Chronicle places it in a half-buried manufactorum canyon at the edge of a failing frontier world. The Muldunine Accord calls it simply “the far corner where mankind had no road left behind it.” Later Legio sermons identify the site as **Aeterion Redoubt**, though no authenticated cartographic record from the period uses that name. It may have been renamed after the fact. Most holy places are.

The purpose was clearer than the location. The great Cineric entity was to be lured into a prepared kill-zone. Immog’s force would hold it. The Proto-Undying apparatus would activate. The entity would be trapped, torn, rendered, dissolved, annihilated, or otherwise prevented from crossing fully into the living order. Immog was to serve as bait and anchor. His life was to be spent buying seconds for a machine whose operators were not certain whether it would kill the beast, worsen it, or open a wound large enough to finish the region.

This must be understood: the First Distillation was not initially a medical breakthrough.

It was a last stand weapon.

The early apparatus is described under many names: the **Aether-Marrow Engine**, the **Cineric Condenser**, the **Muldunine Crown**, the **Proto-Distillatory Mandala**, the **Grave Pressure Array**, the **Salt-Rain Device**, and in one soldier’s diary, “the great iron lung they promised would eat a god if we kept the god still long enough.” The common image preserved across accounts is disturbing in its consistency. The machine heaved. It seemed to breathe. It drew forces no eye could see, including eyes already altered for ash-periphery observation. Instruments failed. Choir members bled from the gums. Liminal witnesses later claimed the air folded inward around a sound too low to hear and too immense not to obey.

Then the unknown force reached into the center of the trap.

The entity began to dissolve.

The Muldunine Accord uses the image that has survived all censorship: **as salt under rain, except the rain flowed upward**.

The beast fought. Some accounts say it tried to flee. Some say it tried to break through Immog and use his living presence as an exit. Some say it attempted to become him. Some say it begged. Those last accounts are generally suppressed because the question of whether Cineric High Lords can beg is considered spiritually unhelpful and politically useless.

What matters is that Immog held it.

He did not defeat it by strength alone. The official Legio version says he fixed the beast in place through disciplined violence, refusing retreat though his armor sloughed and his bones were exposed. The Collegia Mortis version suggests the apparatus required a living anchor with sufficient psychic, biological, and command coherence to prevent the entity from decohering out of the kill-zone. The Cultus Vitae version says he offered his life and his life was refused by death. The Didam version says he denied a rightful eater its meal and therefore cursed all noble hunger thereafter.

The Custodia version, preserved only in summary, states that Immog’s continued presence allowed “hostile essence to coalesce along a human index.”

That sentence has never made anyone safer.

## VI. The Golden Drop

When the apparatus completed its work, the Cineric entity was gone.

In its place hung a drop.

All accounts agree on the drop. They disagree on its size, light, sound, and theological meaning. It is called gold, amber, white-gold, sun-black, marrow-bright, corpse-honey, molten oath, condensed victory, stolen godblood, and the first visible mercy of a universe that had never shown mercy before. It hung suspended in the air at the center of the deactivated apparatus. It was not ordinary liquid. It possessed surface tension against gravity, light against darkness, weight against instruments, and presence against memory. Some witnesses forgot the battle but remembered the drop. Some remembered the drop but forgot their names.

The first distillate had formed.

The machine, having completed its destructive task, released its field.

The drop fell onto Immog.

It did not spill. It entered.

Here the accounts become nearly impossible to read without either laughter, nausea, prayer, or professional detachment. The substance is said to have flowed into him as blood flows out of a mortal body, a tide in reverse, rushing through wounds, mouth, eyes, nostrils, ruptured organs, torn tissue, and every opening made by battle. It did not coat him. It invaded him. It did not heal him politely. It rewrote the terms by which injury was allowed to remain.

The scream that followed is one of the most censored descriptions in Concordial history.

The Muldunine Accord says only: “I learned then that pain has rooms beneath the rooms men know.”

The Seven Ashen Homilies claim that every soldier present fell to one knee because they heard in Immog’s scream all future graves refusing closure. The Iron Chronicle says several Proto-Undying technicians died instantly when their auditory implants attempted to archive the sound. The Varric Rolls state that battlefield animals, machine servitors, injured men, and dead bodies all turned toward him. A banned Didamic commentary insists that the scream was not Immog’s, but the devoured High Lord crying from inside a human vessel.

What emerged was not the man who entered.

Immog had been a powerful individual before the event. No account portrays him as slight. But after the saturation he was described as enlarged beyond ordinary human proportion, near twice the mass of a normal man in some traditions, his body swollen not with fat but with corded force, muscle like cable, skin marked by internal radiance, nerves alive with crackling aetheric discharge, wounds closing around light as if flesh had learned urgency from fire. Later images exaggerate him into a giant. Earlier records are more troubling because they are less grand. They describe a man still recognizably human whose humanity had been made too much of itself.

He breathed.

He stood.

The soldiers around him did not know whether to cheer, flee, or shoot.

This uncertainty is the true birth of the Concord.

Not the victory.

The uncertainty after it.

## VII. First Death After First Life

The transformation did not make Immog invincible in the simple sense used by children, tavern-liars, and noble portraiture.

In the early years after the First Distillation he could be defeated. He could be wounded, broken, pierced, crushed, burned, drowned, and brought low. There are many accounts of him falling in battle. His bodyguards screamed. His adherents protested. Medical officers attempted restraint. Proto-Undying observers demanded examination. Priests attempted to declare the moment holy before knowing whether he would survive it.

Immog rose.

Again and again.

Each return altered politics more than flesh. Soldiers who watched commanders die and remain dead began attaching themselves to the commander who did not. Populations whose worlds had been abandoned by earlier rulers began sending petitions to the man who seemed willing to spend his body as if it were a renewable fortification. Officers who had mocked him as a battlefield curiosity began calculating the consequences of a military leader who could lose duels and still win succession by refusing to remain killed.

The official Legio doctrine says Immog’s repeated battlefield returns proved that the living need not kneel to the dead. The Concordia Logistica doctrine says his survivals forced the creation of a new administrative category because ordinary mortality law could not process command continuity through repeated death-events. The Collegia Mortis doctrine says his body became the first proof that stabilized Cineric essence could bind biological systems against ordinary necrological departure. The Cultus Vitae doctrine says he was permitted to remain because the living required a shield.

The Muldunine Accord is colder.

It states: “The first time I rose, they called it miracle. The second time, they called it asset. The third, they began deciding who should own the method.”

From that sentence flows the Continuity Wars.

## VIII. The Sarantaran Order and the Leaders Who Misread the Miracle

Before Immog’s ascendancy, humanity in his region was governed by councils, noble compacts, military directorates, industrial patrons, and old legal houses later collectively remembered under the name **Sarantaran Order** or **Phylum Sarantares**. The term may have referred to a ruling coalition, a hereditary administrative caste, a military-legal philosophy, or a specific line of high governors whose archives were deliberately thinned after their defeat. The modern Null Court refuses to define the term too narrowly because too many surviving noble houses trace inconvenient legitimacy to one branch or another.

What matters is that the Sarantaran rulers did not initially recognize Immog as the future leader of humanity.

Why would they?

The era already contained strange weapons, modified warriors, half-mechanical patrons, ash-sensitive choirs, necrological surgeons, and men who survived things that should have killed them for several unpleasant hours. Immog’s transformation was extraordinary, but extraordinary things were common enough that rulers could mistake the first immortal for a useful anomaly. They saw a process to refine. They saw a weapon to replicate. They saw a body made valuable by accident. They saw a soldier whose loyalty could be rewarded, managed, bounded, surrounded by contracts, studied under license, and then exceeded by better candidates.

They were wrong.

They were wrong not because Immog was stronger than they thought, though he was. They were wrong because they assumed the First Distillation had created a tool. It had instead created a political event with a sword in its hand.

The Sarantaran administrations sought to repeat the saturation process upon individuals more governable than Immog. Some accounts say they selected noble heirs, condemned prisoners, obedient officers, vat-grown hosts, dying industrial patrons, and machine-house volunteers willing to be molded by the ruling structure. The Proto-Undying Lords saw opportunity. The early Collegia precursors saw data. The governing councils saw continuity under their own hand.

Immog saw disposability.

He had served as bait and survived. Others, he learned, were to be fed into refinements not because they chose sacrifice but because powerful men had discovered that sacrifice could be processed. He had watched worlds spent. He had watched soldiers counted like powder. He had watched the poor evacuated after machines and ledgers. He had seen enough of humanity treated as raw input. The method that had saved him was already becoming another instrument by which rulers would consume those beneath them to preserve those above.

The Muldunine Accord says he did not rebel immediately.

That is important.

A saint would have rebelled at once in later sermons. A tyrant would have seized power at first opportunity in enemy histories. Immog did neither. He gathered systems. He secured loyal formations. He cultivated industrial allies who preferred his order to Sarantaran uncertainty. He negotiated with Proto-Undying houses that wished to survive whichever side won. He made himself indispensable along breach fronts. He allowed old rulers to believe they were using him until enough soldiers, worlds, foundries, and ration chains had learned to look toward him before looking toward their lawful masters.

Then he declared them enemies of humankind.

The phrase survives in too many lineages to be invention.

It is also the first example of Immog’s most dangerous habit: he did not merely defeat rivals. He placed them outside the moral shelter of humanity and made their destruction an act of preservation.

Every hidden arm of the Concord learned from this.

## IX. The Marshaling

The period between Immog’s declaration against the Sarantaran order and the open Continuity Wars is called **the Marshaling**.

The name is deceptively orderly. It suggests banners, musters, supply ledgers, speeches, and marching columns. It was those things. It was also assassinations, oath inversions, foundry seizures, record corrections, noble hostage exchanges, breach-line betrayals, temple recognitions, ration diversions, and the quiet conversion of local necessity into empire-scale allegiance.

Immog understood, or learned quickly, that immortality alone does not govern. A man may rise after death and still lose a city because the grain elevators answer to someone else. He therefore took not merely fortresses but schedules, fuel allotments, corpse registries, transit corridors, manufactorum patents, and the loyalty of those who knew how to keep lamps burning after heroic speeches ended. The Concordia Logistica traces its philosophical ancestry to this period, though it prefers to speak of continuity administration rather than the less comfortable truth that bureaucracy was one of Immog’s earliest weapons.

The Eternus Legio also formed in this crucible. What had been military force became civil spine. Legions swore not only to fight enemies but to keep human continuity standing against death, ash, hunger, and disorder. Their oath was beautiful when spoken over saved cities and hideous when used to shoot civilians in ration riots. Both uses appear early.

The Proto-Undying Lords supplied engines, siege arrays, preservation chambers, field distillers, surgical regrowth frames, and the technical means by which Immog’s first experiments in distributed Draught could proceed. In return they received license, protection, access to Cineric residues, and legal darkness. Their later power did not emerge as corruption after a pure founding. It was built into the founding because Immog needed them and they knew it.

The Cultus Vitae began not as a unified church but as a constellation of grave-refusal cults, battlefield chaplaincies, family continuance rites, anti-Cineric household practices, and civic mourning disciplines that found common language around Immog’s defiance of death. The poor accepted its consolations because it buried their dead properly and fed their children in breach shelters. The powerful accepted it because it taught suffering to stand in formation.

The Collegia Mortis grew out of those who could examine the impossible without immediately worshipping or fleeing it. They were needed to measure distillate, stabilize it, prevent uncontrolled Reversion, classify corpse-risk, and determine whether the substance falling from slain Cineric entities could be medicine, weapon, sacrament, ration, poison, or all of these depending on dilution and law.

The Marshaling made Immog ruler before he was crowned by any formal act.

By the time the old order named him rebel, whole districts had already begun writing his orders into their daily schedules.

## X. The Great Distillation Changed Because Immog Feared the First Drop

The First Distillation placed an entire condensed Cineric essence into one body.

Immog never repeated that act in the same form.

This restraint has been interpreted many ways. Devotional accounts say he refused to make another suffer as he had suffered. Legio accounts say the strategic risk of producing another being equal to the Founder was unacceptable during civil war. Collegia accounts say the first event depended upon unique conditions impossible to reproduce safely: a sufficiently vast Cineric entity, a living anchor of unusual coherence, a crude apparatus whose violence created unrepeatable metaphysical pressure, and a battlefield collapse whose instability paradoxically permitted saturation. Noble accounts say he hoarded the secret. Didamic accounts say he stole a feast and then rationed crumbs to ensure obedience.

The Muldunine Accord says: “I had been remade by a mercy shaped like violation. I would not drop a god into another man and call the convulsion policy.”

Instead, the ritual changed.

Distillation became distribution.

The Draught emerged not as another golden drop poured entire into a chosen vessel, but as fractionalized, stabilized, chemically mediated Cineric Vitae distributed among many recipients in controlled amounts. The process preserved, restored, extended, reinforced, and addicted. It could prolong the lives of commanders, heal catastrophic wounds, sustain noble bodies long past natural decay, reinforce elite soldiers, stabilize ash-exposed specialists, and maintain the continuity of institutions whose leaders claimed they could not be replaced without civil collapse.

This was the second founding crime.

The first was discovering that the dead could be rendered.

The second was discovering that the rendered dead could be rationed.

Once rationed, it became economy. Once economy, it became hierarchy. Once hierarchy, it became law. Once law, it became morality. Once morality, it became nearly impossible to admit that the entire arrangement had begun with one screaming man soaked in the condensed remains of a defeated horror.

## XI. The Draught as Medicine, Sacrament, Industry, and Lie

Modern citizens know the Draught primarily through rumor. Nobles drink it from sealed ampules. Legates receive it under battlefield writ. The dying dream of it. The poor curse it. The Cultus Vitae denies that ordinary devotional materials contain meaningful concentrations while simultaneously blessing scented candles whose supply chains run too close to Collegia Mortis subsidiaries for comfort. The Collegia insists that fractional Cineric Vitae indices are medically distinct from noble Draught allotments. This is technically true and morally evasive.

Fractional amounts of stabilized distillate appear in the most potent restoratives of Concordial medicine. A few drops, a few micrograms, or less than visible measure, chemically acted upon by binder reagents, marrow salts, ash-stable solvents, or sacral stabilizers, can amplify healing beyond ordinary pharmacology. Bone regrowth accelerates. Organ shock reverses. Infection retreats. Tissue remembers intended shape. Shocked nerves return to signal. Patients whose death-status had entered preliminary filing sometimes remain inconveniently alive.

These concentrations are not sufficient to satisfy the gorging need of the noble houses.

That distinction is important.

A medical ampule containing fractional distillate does not make a worker immortal. It buys time, function, repair, and sometimes debt. A noble Draught service, by contrast, is a continuity regime built around repeated access, lineage privilege, body preparation, legal entitlement, and networks of Collegia oversight. To compare the two is like comparing a candle to a furnace because both involve flame.

And yet the candle matters.

Rumor persists that holy fragrances, funerary incense, and devotional scented candles of the Cultus Vitae sometimes contain a single particle, trace index, or symbolic dilution of distillate. The Cultus describes such claims as misunderstanding of blessing chemistry. The Collegia describes them as uncontrolled folk pharmacology. The Concordia describes them as procurement-sensitive. Noble houses describe them as blasphemous waste when poor parishes receive supply and as spiritually correct when their own private chapels do.

The people burn them by the thousands.

They do so because the chance of breathing even one particle of preserved victory is enough to make hunger kneel for a moment. They do so because a mother with a fevered child will light every candle she can steal if someone whispers that one in ten thousand might carry a mote of the First Distillation’s mercy. They do so because humans are not ledgers, no matter how hard the Concordia has tried to improve them.

In lower-stack shrines the wax guttering of a suspected Vitae candle is scraped, saved, diluted, rubbed onto scars, mixed into broth, pressed into the lips of the dying, or sold in fraud markets under names too holy to prosecute without causing riots. Most of it is ordinary wax, perfume, ash, tallow, and desperation.

Desperation remains the Concord’s most widely distributed sacrament.

## XII. Immog and the Noble Houses

Immog did not create nobility from nothing. He inherited noble houses, charter dynasts, war families, industrial patrons, military bloodlines, transit owners, land-rights holders, corpse-rights holders, and old families whose legitimacy consisted mainly of having survived long enough to write favorable genealogies. What he did was bind them to continuity.

The Ashbourne and related noble orders later claimed that Immog purified aristocracy by requiring service in exchange for privilege. This is a charitable reading. Less polished ledgers show a more violent bargain. Houses that supplied ships, troops, food, laboratories, choir lines, corpse-processing rights, or political obedience were preserved, elevated, or re-chartered. Houses that resisted were extinguished, absorbed, renamed, or written into treason rolls so thoroughly that descendants still petition to prove they are not related to people whose names no longer officially exist.

The Draught transformed noble politics. Before distillation, a house could endure through inheritance. After distillation, a house could be governed by the same hungry elder for centuries. Continuity became personality. Grudges became infrastructure. Legal memory became biological fact. The old died more slowly, surrendered less readily, and learned to treat younger generations as replaceable limbs rather than successors.

Immog appears to have understood the danger early.

He needed long-lived commanders, stable administrators, veteran industrial patrons, and specialists who could remember practices no archive could safely hold. He also feared creating a class whose access to continued life would make them view ordinary humanity as seasonal. His solution was characteristic: he did not abolish the danger. He organized it, licensed it, taxed it, sanctified it, placed soldiers around it, placed priests beside it, placed clerks beneath it, and later placed hidden knives behind it.

The Custodia Veritatis, Null Court, Ferrum Sanctum, Cohors Noctis, and other hidden arms all claim some ancestry in Immog’s recognition that immortal power must be watched by institutions cruel enough to frighten it.

This is one reason nobles praise him in public and curse him in private.

He gave them centuries.

He also gave those centuries paperwork, auditors, executioners, ration claims, genealogical traps, and laws by which a house may be declared dead while most of its members are still breathing.

## XIII. Immog and the Proto-Undying Lords

No account of Immog can be separated from the Proto-Undying Lords.

They made the apparatus possible. They understood pressure, containment, corpse-resonance, marrow chemistry, grief conductivity, memory lattice behavior, field harmonics, and the obscene engineering required to reach into the Cinerum without merely opening a door for everything that wanted in. Without them, Immog likely dies as bait and the beast either escapes or devours the battlefield. Without Immog, they likely continue refining the device through expendable subjects until something worse than a founder emerges.

Their alliance was necessity disguised as destiny.

The Proto-Undying Lords wanted mechanical continuance, preservation of knowledge, removal of fleshly weakness, and technologies that could make mortality an engineering problem. Immog wanted humanity to survive the dead. These goals overlapped just enough to build civilization and diverged enough to poison it forever.

The Muldunine Accord describes them with bitterness but not contempt. It calls them “men who loved the future so fiercely they ceased to notice the people living in it.” This phrase remains banned in several machine-house courts because workers adopted it as a strike chant during the Ninth Furnace Petitions.

The first distillation devices were not holy objects. They were machines designed by desperate genius under military pressure. Their operators argued while soldiers died. Their containment seals failed. Their calculations used corpse-reaction data gathered under conditions no modern ethics seal would admit except under emergency writ, and emergency writ is famously generous once an institution learns to enjoy it. The Collegia Mortis later purified the language. The Ferrum Sanctum later claimed doctrinal lineage. The Undying Lords later treated their role as proof that machine aristocracy saved mankind.

They did help save mankind.

This is why judging them cleanly is difficult.

They also taught mankind to industrialize the dead.

This is why forgiving them cleanly is impossible.

## XIV. The First Immortal as Military Problem

An immortal commander is not merely a commander who does not die.

He is a command problem that never vacates office naturally.

Early Legio records preserve admiration, terror, resentment, and fatigue in equal measure. Immog would enter battle with nary a thought for his own safety, not because he lacked tactical sense, but because his personal risk calculus had been rewritten. He could spend himself where others could not. He could anchor a line by presence alone. He could rally broken troops because his survival made retreat seem cowardly and because men ashamed by an immortal’s example often mistake shame for courage.

This made him useful.

It also made him dangerous to every officer beneath him.

How does one advise a man who has died more often than one has been promoted? How does one restrain a commander whose body can absorb failure but whose army cannot? How does one argue casualty projections with someone whose own death no longer appears on the same ledger? How does one succession-plan around a leader who has become evidence against succession?

The answer was the Eternus Legio.

Not all at once. Not cleanly. But over time Immog’s personal continuity was translated into institutional continuity. The Legio became the wall that could stand even when individuals fell, because its founder had demonstrated the political power of standing again. Its doctrine teaches that the living will not kneel to the dead, but beneath that motto lies a more complicated lesson: the living must build structures that can survive the death of those who lead them, even when those leaders refuse to demonstrate proper mortality.

This contradiction remains unresolved.

Every Legate who invokes Immog must decide whether to imitate his courage or correct his recklessness. Most choose both, loudly, while ordering others to pay for the difference.

## XV. Founder Devotions of the Cultus Vitae

The Cultus Vitae venerates Immog as the Grave-Defier, though senior doctrinal custodians are careful to insist that veneration is not worship. The distinction satisfies theologians, alarms the Custodia, and means very little to lower-stack families who light Grave-Defier candles under rusted icons showing a huge armored figure standing between a cradle and a wall of ash.

The Cultus needs Immog.

Without him, the central doctrine of lawful continuity becomes colder. He is the proof-story that the living may resist the False Beyond without surrendering to the Didam’s hunger, Nezram’s grief, the Unburdened’s flesh-hatred, or the Undying Lords’ dream of replacing man with machine. He is presented as flawed so that obedience remains possible despite visible institutional sin. He is presented as necessary so that dissent may be framed as ingratitude. He is presented as suffering so that the poor may imagine their suffering participates in the same defense.

The oldest Grave-Defier homilies are harsher than modern chapel versions. They do not promise that Immog was pure. They promise that purity is less useful than standing in the breach. They do not say immortality is holy. They say it becomes lawful only when chained to service. They do not say death was conquered. They say death was made to labor and must never again be allowed to command unopposed.

Among the poor, Immog is invoked differently. A father going to corpse duty may mutter Immog’s name not because he loves the Concord but because he wants to return. A mother may press a wax scrap from a suspected Vitae candle under her child’s tongue and ask the Grave-Defier to share a fraction of the refusal. A condemned soldier may curse Immog for teaching commanders that bodies can be spent twice. An Unentered elder may spit at his icon and still shelter beneath a Cultus breach awning funded by offerings made in that same icon’s name.

This is proper Concordial religion: faith, resentment, hunger, fear, gratitude, and logistics sharing the same room while the roof leaks ash.

## XVI. Founder Law of the Concordia Logistica

To the Concordia Logistica, Immog is less a hero than the first successful argument for systems larger than sentiment.

His survivals broke existing categories. Dead commanders could not retain office. Living commanders could not inherit from their own death estates. Resurrection, if that word may be used before its legal containment, produced unbearable confusion around pay, command rights, marital continuity, rank seniority, injury compensation, ration entitlement, and culpability for orders issued before a death-event and executed after return.

The Concordia’s earliest founder-law fragments are therefore not poetic. They concern whether Immog owed back taxes during periods when he had been pronounced dead by field surgeons, whether soldiers who abandoned his fallen body were guilty of desertion if the body later resumed command, whether equipment issued to him during one life-state remained issued after the next, and whether a battlefield death interrupted an oath.

From these ugly questions grew continuity law.

The Concordia claims that Immog understood this necessity. The Muldunine Accord supports them in part. He saw that survival required more than valor. It required records. If a city cannot know who owns a pump, the pump fails. If a pump fails, children die. If children die, grief feeds the Cinerum. If grief feeds the Cinerum, the breach widens. If the breach widens, soldiers die. If soldiers die, histories praise courage while everyone remaining drinks recycled despair.

Therefore ledgers matter.

This is the Concordia’s favorite moral chain.

It is true enough to be terrifying.

Immog’s legacy gave the Concordia permission to make identity, ration, death, and survival matters of civil war. Their clerks still cite founder necessity when denying petitions. A widow whose spouse returned under Draught stabilization after legal death may wait years for marital continuity correction. A district declared Mortis Pending may be stripped of future ration expectations because Immog’s founding decrees permit pre-death allocation during strategic emergency. A child born in an unregistered breach shelter may be assigned provisional identity under forms descended from battlefield categories originally designed for Immog’s impossible body.

No one is crushed more efficiently than someone crushed by a form that once solved a real problem.

## XVII. Collegia Mortis Interpretation: The First Host

The Collegia Mortis teaches its senior distillers that Immog was not merely the first recipient of Cineric Vitae. He was the first host to survive total unmediated saturation by condensed hostile essence without immediate Reversion, possession, conceptual fragmentation, or terminal liquefaction.

Students are not permitted to romanticize this.

The golden drop did not bless him. It entered a ruptured biological system under battlefield conditions while the apparatus field collapsed. It encountered tissue, blood, nerve, marrow, memory, command identity, pain, will, fear, and whatever metaphysical binding keeps a living person attached to the world. It should have killed him. It should have opened him. It should have turned him into a breach-organ, a walking regnant seed, a Didamic feast-source, or a screaming statue of marrow. Instead, it stabilized.

The Collegia has spent thousands of years trying to understand why.

Theories include unusual neural coherence, battlefield oath-density, ash-exposure conditioning, genetic compatibility, Proto-Undying pre-treatment, accidental field geometry, the entity’s weakened state, intervention by unknown Liminal choir harmonics, or the possibility that Immog did die and something continuous enough to be administratively recognized as Immog resumed operation afterward.

That last theory is not taught below senior rank.

The First Host problem informs all modern Draught work. Distillate must be fractioned because whole-host saturation remains catastrophically unsafe. Stabilization reagents must be used because raw Vitae behaves like condensed desire under pressure. Recipients must be screened because their grief, ambition, hunger, bloodline, and institutional role affect integration. Overuse creates noble gorge syndrome, Reversion susceptibilities, and metaphysical appetite loops. Underuse wastes material extracted at cost no honest ledger can bear.

The Collegia’s founder motto for this archive is preserved in a black classroom beneath a furnace library:

**Do not worship the first success. Determine why it failed to become disaster.**

The fact that the Concord has often done the opposite is not considered a useful topic for junior instruction.

## XVIII. Enemy Interpretations

The Didam of Zaltech call Immog the Thief of the Feast.

Their doctrine holds that the Cineric High Lord slain at the First Distillation was not a beast to be destroyed but a rightful eater in the sacred chain of consumption. By rendering it and taking its essence into human continuity, Immog committed the first theft against hunger. The Draught, in Didamic interpretation, is cowardly cannibalism: the nobles drink death while pretending not to feast. They do not object to consumption. They object to law pretending consumption is medicine when nobles do it and heresy when starving mouths do.

This accusation is strategically inconvenient because it contains a shard of truth.

Nezram’s Mourning Court calls Immog the Deaf King. To them, the First Distillation was not victory but refusal to hear the dead properly. The scream of the entity, the scream of Immog, and the screams of all later Draught subjects are considered one continuous note suppressed by Concordial doctrine. The Last Cry cults teach that Immog conquered death by making himself unable to mourn it. Their rites attempt to make his deafness fail.

This accusation is spiritually dangerous because grief is one of the Cinerum’s easiest roads.

The Unburdened call him the Chainmaker. They claim he saw that flesh is a prison and chose to reinforce the prison with stolen afterworld force rather than release humanity from the burden of bodies. In their telling, the Concord is a slaughterhouse built to keep souls attached to meat long enough for rulers to profit.

This accusation is metaphysically infectious because despair often finds anti-flesh doctrine comfortable.

The Heliun remnants, where such remnants are suspected, treat Immog as an obsolete biological tyrant whose continuance remained trapped in mass, pain, and scarcity. To digitalist heresy, the First Distillation proved only that humans will commit atrocity to preserve bodies when mind could have multiplied beyond them.

This accusation is machine-forbidden because the Heliun Divide demonstrated what multiplication without mass can do to civilization.

Each enemy preserves part of Immog more honestly than his devotees do.

This is why enemy archives are sealed rather than destroyed.

A civilization that burns all hostile testimony eventually learns only its own excuses.

## XIX. Physical Descriptions and the Problem of Founder Iconography

No stable image of Immog survives without dispute.

The oldest battlefield descriptions portray a large man before the First Distillation and a larger, altered figure after it. He is described as corded, scarred, heat-bright, ash-stained, electrically wreathed, gold-eyed in some accounts, black-eyed in others, with skin sometimes described as bronze, grey, burned, pale, or “the color of a storm seen from beneath water.” Later noble portraits make him beautiful in the way rulers prefer founders to be beautiful. Lower-stack icons make him broad, faceless, and protective. Legio banners make him armored beyond anatomy. Cultus devotional candles show him with one hand raised against ash and the other extended toward the living. Collegia diagrams reduce him to cross-sections, saturation channels, marrow uptake arrows, and marginal warnings.

The Custodia prohibits any single official face.

This prohibition prevents several dangers. If a true face were declared, forged relics would multiply. If a false face were declared, those who possessed older images would gain destabilizing leverage. If no face is declared, every institution may maintain its useful Immog while admitting, silently, that certainty has been denied them.

Looking upon early post-distillation images is reported to cause unease in sensitive observers. Whether this is memetic contamination, devotional expectation, poor preservation chemistry, or the human tendency to fear a face associated with too many deaths remains undetermined. The Liminal Office notes that ash-tuned individuals sometimes weep black tears when exposed to certain Founder plates. The Collegia believes this response may indicate residual Cineric indexing. The Cultus calls it recognition. The Ferrum Sanctum calls it unhelpful moisture.

Modern statues deliberately exaggerate armor and reduce flesh. The Concord prefers Immog as institution. Flesh invites questions. Armor receives salutes.

## XX. The Continuity Wars

The Continuity Wars began as conflict over who would control the method of survival and became conflict over what survival meant.

The Sarantaran order and its allies sought to contain Immog, replicate the process, and preserve pre-existing hierarchies. Immog’s coalition sought to bring distillation, military command, industrial supply, civil law, and anti-Cineric doctrine under a new order defined by continuity against the dead. Noble houses joined whichever side seemed likely to preserve their estates. Proto-Undying factions split according to technical access, philosophy, rivalry, and fear. Early Cultus movements followed miracles, kitchens, grave-rights, and whoever kept children from being processed as corpse-risk. Entire worlds changed allegiance after one shipment of medicine arrived before one shipment of soldiers.

The wars were not continuous in the simple sense. They were campaigns, purges, treaties, reversals, local civil collapses, breach emergencies, noble feuds, machine-house sieges, mutinies, and decades of arguments conducted with artillery. They created the modern map less by conquest than by exhaustion. Those who survived learned to prefer a cruel order that kept the walls sealed to a lawful order that arrived three weeks after the ash did.

Immog’s personal role grew larger as the wars lengthened. He was battlefield commander, symbol, judge, executioner, negotiator, hostage guarantor, oath recipient, and living proof that the Draught was not merely myth. His enemies tried to kill him. Some succeeded temporarily. His allies tried to manage him. Some succeeded temporarily. Populations tried to worship him. He sometimes allowed it when useful and condemned it when inconvenient. This inconsistency became theology.

The Continuity Wars also produced the first great distillation regimes. Cineric entities slain in battle were processed when possible. High-value residues were stabilized. Fractional Vitae was routed to critical commanders, founders of future noble houses, medical corps, and experimental recipients. The first debates over who deserved continuance began before the wars were won. No civilization waits until peace to begin becoming unfair.

By the end, the old order was broken. Immog’s order stood.

It called itself preservation.

Its enemies called it conquest.

The records support both.

## XXI. The First Distiller’s Disappearance

No issue in Founder studies produces more dangerous speculation than Immog’s final state.

He may have died. He may have withdrawn. He may have fragmented. He may have been interred in a black continuity engine beneath an unnamed Turris foundation. He may command through successors carrying partial mnemonic grafts. He may persist as a Cineric-adjacent entity bound to the Concord’s survival architecture. He may have become the first successful case of human-to-institutional transference. He may have been murdered by those who understood that no civilization can mature while its founder remains in the room.

The official public doctrine is carefully noncommittal. Immog is spoken of as Founder, not current ruler. His commands are historical, not immediate. His presence is devotional, not administrative. Yet sealed Legio protocols still contain contingencies labeled **AETER RETURN**, **FOUNDER HOSTILITY**, **FALSE AETER CLAIMANT**, **AETERIC ECHO EVENT**, and **PRIMARY CONTINUANCE RECOGNITION FAILURE**. The Custodia refuses to confirm or deny whether these are active files. Their refusal has been interpreted as proof by every faction already determined to be wrong in its preferred direction.

The Muldunine Accord complicates matters by claiming to have been written thousands of years after the events it describes. If authentic, Immog lived long enough to regret at length. If forged, the forger had access to details later verified by sealed material. If produced by mnemonic reconstruction, the question becomes whether a memory admitting guilt is legally the same person as the actor who committed it. Null Court benches have killed petitioners for asking less destabilizing questions.

The disappearance of the First Distiller is also linked, in several restricted theories, to the later creation or hardening of hidden Concordial arms. The Cohors Noctis may have been formalized in the shadow of his absence to ensure the Eternus Legio could be corrected without a living founder. The Custodia Veritatis may have inherited founder-level authority precisely because no single Immog remained available to settle truth. The Null Court’s most extreme powers may descend from emergency founder writs left unsigned but never revoked. The Ferrum Sanctum’s authority over machine continuance may reflect fear that Immog’s own continuity had approached unacceptable forms.

A civilization built by an immortal must eventually answer one question.

What if he leaves?

The Concord answered by becoming many institutions cruel enough to imitate him badly.

## XXII. Relics of Immog

Relics attributed to Immog are common enough to be statistically impossible and politically necessary.

There are blades said to have been carried by him, armor plates blackened by the First Distillation, drops of hardened golden residue sealed in noble reliquaries, fragments of the first apparatus, battlefield stones, oath-scrolls, command seals, bones from bodies that allegedly were his before regeneration, blood-rust scraped from early war banners, and wax impressions of rings no two archives draw the same way.

Most are false.

Some are false but old enough to have become useful.

A few are real enough that the Custodia removes them quietly.

The most dangerous relics are not objects but procedures. A fragmentary process map from the first apparatus can inspire illegal distillers. A battlefield order written in Immog’s name can justify emergency powers. A medical note describing his post-saturation symptoms can guide noble houses toward whole-host experiments. A private letter expressing regret can become a weapon against every institution founded in his shadow.

The Concordia maintains a relic classification system whose categories include **Devotional Fraud**, **Harmless Local Comfort**, **Destabilizing but Nonfunctional**, **Technically Informative**, **Draught-Relevant**, **Founder-Claim Supportive**, **Founder-Claim Contradictory**, and **Immediate Seizure Without Receipt**. That last category is the largest by mass despite having the fewest acknowledged entries.

Lower-stack markets sell Immog’s tears in glass beads, Immog’s ash in paper twists, Immog’s marrow in illegal ampules, Immog’s breath in sealed reliquary whistles, and Immog’s blessing in candle smoke. Most kill only by ordinary contamination. Occasionally one contains a true fractional distillate particle stolen from a chapel supply or medical waste stream.

Those rare cases keep the fraud economy alive.

Hope is the most durable counterfeit.

## XXIII. On Founder Guilt

The Muldunine Accord is obsessed with guilt.

This distinguishes it from most official founder texts, which prefer necessity. Necessity is tidy. It explains without apologizing. It allows institutions to say there was no other choice and then continue enjoying the benefits of the choice. Guilt is less useful. Guilt remains warm in the hand. It asks who paid, who profited, who remembers, and who has converted remorse into ceremony rather than correction.

If the Accord is authentic, Immog condemned many of his own decisions. He did not condemn survival. He condemned what survival became when powerful men learned to drink it. He condemned the conversion of emergency distillation into hereditary entitlement. He condemned experiments performed on the disposable. He condemned himself for allowing useful cruelty to become permanent because undoing it during war seemed impossible and undoing it after war seemed destabilizing.

The Accord’s most quoted forbidden passage reads:

**I taught mankind that death could be made to serve. I did not teach it quickly enough that service is not slavery without consequence. Now every lord who drinks calls himself necessary, every clerk who denies a ration calls himself continuity, every soldier who fires into a crowd calls himself the wall, and every corpse we process asks me whether victory was merely hunger with a banner.**

This passage is banned in noble schools, restricted in Legio officer academies, and recited privately by certain Cultus Vitae confessors to those who have ordered necessary evils and survived the ordering.

The Concord has never known what to do with a founder who may have repented without dismantling what he built.

So it does what Concordial institutions do best.

It files the repentance, seals the file, quotes useful fragments, prosecutes unauthorized context, and continues.

## XXIV. The Founder and the Poor

For most citizens, Immog is not an archive problem. He is a shape in smoke, a name on ration oaths, a statue in a transit hall, a curse muttered when soldiers pass, a promise painted over a clinic door, a candle label, a children’s breach-song, or the reason some noble still breathes after three hundred years while a worker dies of coolant lung before forty.

The poor know contradictions faster than scholars.

They know Immog saved humanity because without the Legio the ash would have eaten the spires. They know Immog damned humanity because without the Draught the high houses might have remembered how to die. They know the Cultus feeds orphans in his name. They know the Concordia denies appeals in his name. They know soldiers rescue districts in his name. They know soldiers quarantine districts in his name. They know holy candles might contain nothing. They burn them anyway.

Lower-stack songs rarely call him Aeter. They call him Old Immog, Big Immog, Ash-Bait, Candle-Father, First Screamer, Dead-Man-Standing, the Tall One, the One What Got Up, or simply Him. These names are technically improper. They are also the reason the Founder remains emotionally available to people who would spit on a noble lecture about continuity.

One common lower-stack saying is preserved here because repeated suppression has failed:

**Immog stole death from the dead and the highborn stole it from him.**

The Cultus considers this theologically immature.

The Custodia considers it politically sensitive.

The Concordia considers it vandalism when painted on pump housings.

The sentence persists because it is short enough to write before patrols arrive.

## XXV. The Founder and the Ash

Liminal testimony concerning Immog is rare, heavily censored, and unpleasant.

Those capable of tuning their minds toward the Ashen Sea often report founder-resonance as a pressure rather than an image. Some describe a vast standing heat, not warm enough to comfort and not cold enough to ignore. Others describe a golden downward wound through which ash moves upward. Victor-grade Liminal navigators have claimed that certain routes through the black drifts become briefly clearer when the name Immog is spoken in old battle cadence, though the Liminal Office refuses to approve this as navigation procedure due to unacceptable devotional contamination of pilot practice.

There are also reports of black tears among Liminal sensitives exposed to First Distillation relics or early apparatus diagrams. The official explanation is ash-resonance discharge through altered biological ducts. The unofficial explanation among choir crews is that the Ash remembers the first theft and presses against those who can hear it.

If Immog remains in any form, the Liminal Office does not say where.

One sealed memorandum, quoted only through Custodia paraphrase, warns that “Founder-resonance should not be mistaken for Founder-presence, Founder-presence should not be mistaken for Founder-intent, and Founder-intent should not be solicited unless the petitioner has already accepted responsibility for the collapse of all derivative authority structures.”

This is the polite way of saying: do not pray too successfully.

## XXVI. Counterfeit Immogs and Founder Claimants

Every few generations someone claims to be Immog returned.

Most are madmen, cult leaders, noble frauds, ash-touched veterans, Draught-distorted heirs, machine-house puppets, or desperate political actors betting that a sufficiently unstable region will obey a founder faster than a governor. A few have been difficult enough to require hidden investigation before public disposal. At least three claimants demonstrated anomalous regeneration. One carried memories of battles not publicly recorded. One spoke an obsolete command dialect that caused two Legio cohorts to hesitate. One caused a Cultus relic candle to burn backward for seven minutes before being shot, sealed, revived, interrogated, shot again, and transferred to Collegia custody.

The Concord maintains the **False Aeter Protocols** for such events.

Publicly, claimants are denounced as frauds. Privately, they are tested against sealed biological, mnemonic, doctrinal, tactical, and Cineric criteria. If they fail, they vanish. If they partially succeed, they vanish more carefully. If they fully succeed, the protocol does not specify a public outcome in any copy available to this archive.

The greatest danger of a claimant is not that he is Immog.

The greatest danger is that people may decide he is useful enough to treat as Immog.

Founders need not be authentic once armies begin moving.

## XXVII. On the Distribution of the Founder Myth Among Institutions

Each institution maintains its own Immog because a single Immog would condemn too many of them.

The Eternus Legio keeps Immog the commander: wounded, resolute, immense, returning from death to hold the line. This Immog authorizes courage, discipline, sacrifice, and command continuity.

The Concordia Logistica keeps Immog the system-builder: the man who learned that survival requires records, rationing, corpse accountability, transit control, and identities durable enough to outlast chaos. This Immog authorizes bureaucracy.

The Collegia Mortis keeps Immog the First Host: the proof that Cineric essence can be rendered, stabilized, and made medically or strategically useful. This Immog authorizes distillation.

The Cultus Vitae keeps Immog the Grave-Defier: flawed, suffering, necessary, standing between the living and the False Beyond. This Immog authorizes obedience through consolation.

The noble houses keep Immog the grantor: the founder whose emergency continuance allotments became inherited privilege. This Immog authorizes Draught entitlement.

The Ferrum Sanctum keeps Immog the continuity problem: the biological miracle that proves flesh may be improved but also proves uncontrolled continuance can destabilize civilization. This Immog authorizes machine oversight.

The Custodia Veritatis keeps Immog the dangerous truth: the founder whose actual history could break the stories required to preserve the order he built. This Immog authorizes secrecy.

The Null Court keeps Immog the precedent: the first case in which ordinary law failed and exceptional ruling became civilization. This Immog authorizes terrifying judgments.

The Cohors Noctis keeps Immog the absent check: the founder whose disappearance required knives capable of correcting immortal soldiers. This Immog authorizes hidden murder.

The poor keep Immog the contradiction: savior and thief, candle and chain, the one who got up and the reason others are ordered to stay down.

This last Immog is least official.

It may be closest to the man.

## XXVIII. Medical Indices of First Distillation Contamination

Because the First Distillation remains technically relevant, the Collegia Mortis requires senior personnel to recognize probable symptoms of uncontrolled high-grade distillate exposure based on founder accounts and subsequent failed replications.

Symptoms include rapid closure of lethal wounds without proportional tissue loss, temporary increase in body mass without sufficient caloric intake, internal luminescence, aetheric discharge along nerve pathways, extreme pain response followed by altered pain interpretation, persistence of command identity after death-state registration, marrow temperature instability, hostility from nearby Cineric residues, attraction of grief phenomena, and memory accretion not belonging to the host.

No modern patient exhibiting more than three founder-pattern symptoms is to be treated as ordinary casualty, noble overdose, sainted survivor, or legal person without emergency Collegia review.

This instruction exists because previous staff attempted compassion.

Compassion remains permitted.

Unreviewed compassion near a possible founder-pattern saturation event is punishable by furnace reassignment, memory audit, or execution depending on how many walls begin whispering afterward.

## XXIX. Economic Consequences of Founder Distillation

The First Distillation created more than immortality.

It created the most valuable substance in Concordial history.

Before the Draught, power rested on land, ships, armies, manufactories, food, water, fuel, weapons, records, and the ability to prevent the dead from entering one’s bedroom. After the Draught, power also rested on access to extended time. A ruler who lives two centuries accumulates advantage no ordinary heir can match. A technician preserved for three hundred years becomes a walking archive. A noble who controls a distillate route controls not merely wealth but the life expectancy of rivals. A military order with Draught allotments can preserve commanders through campaigns that would otherwise devour institutional memory.

The economy bent.

Cineric hunting became strategic industry. Corpse law tightened because death-residue had new value and new danger. Noble marriages began considering Draught compatibility. Medical guilds reorganized around fractional access. The Cultus developed doctrines distinguishing lawful preservation from hunger. The Didam grew more persuasive wherever people saw nobles drink what the poor were forbidden to taste. The Concordia built ledgers so complex that entire families became lost inside eligibility categories. The Collegia Mortis became indispensable and therefore suspicious. Smuggling markets emerged before the first official monopoly was complete.

The Concord claims this was necessary.

It may have been.

Necessity is still capable of building a throne.

## XXX. The First Distiller as Industrial Founder

Immog is often remembered as soldier or immortal, but the more enduring part of his rule may be industrial.

A single miraculous survivor cannot feed a spire. A battlefield victory cannot repair a pump. A founder’s scream cannot machine valve tolerances. Immog’s genius, if that word is permitted, lay in understanding that the miracle had to become process or it would become relic. He forced alliances between soldiers, clerks, priests, corpse-scientists, machine houses, and noble administrators. He turned afterworld violence into an input stream. He turned emergency production into permanent infrastructure. He turned survival into an industry.

This is why his legacy cannot be judged only by intention.

The industrialization of distillation allowed humanity to endure. It also required extraction from the Cinerum, controlled killing of entities, corpse processing, human experimentation, ration hierarchy, and permanent institutions whose survival depends upon continuing access to the thing they claim to control. The Concord’s moral structure is therefore circular. It must continue distillation because it is built around distillation. It must justify being built around distillation because stopping would expose how much else has become dependent.

Immog began this circle.

Whether he meant to close it around civilization’s throat is unknowable.

The circle closed anyway.

## XXXI. The Great Apparatus in Later Memory

The original apparatus of the First Distillation does not survive in acknowledged form.

Fragments are claimed by the Collegia Mortis, the Ferrum Sanctum, several noble houses, two Cultus Vitae fortress-cathedrals, and at least one Liminal black archive. The Custodia states that no complete device exists. This statement is less comforting than intended because the Custodia also states that no unauthorized complete device exists, that no public complete device exists, and that citizens should not concern themselves with distinctions generated by emergency security language.

Later distillation engines are safer, smaller, more controlled, more classified, and more numerous. They do not breathe in the old way, say the engineers. They do not heave like lungs. They do not draw upward rain. They do not require a living immortal to hold a screaming High Lord in place. These reassurances are standard in Collegia procurement sermons.

Technicians who have worked near high-yield distillers often refuse to repeat them.

The apparatus lives in metaphor because no one agrees what its physical survival would mean. To the Legio it was the first siege engine against death. To the Collegia it was the crude ancestor of all lawful distillation. To the Cultus it was the altar of grave-defiance. To the Didam it was the first theft-machine. To the Undying Lords it was proof that sufficient engineering can shame theology. To the Ferrum Sanctum it was a warning that tools must not be allowed to become gods merely because gods can be processed by tools.

To Immog, if the Muldunine Accord is believed, it was “the iron mouth that made me useful forever.”

No machine-house likes that sentence.

## XXXII. Founder Contradictions Preserved for Officer Instruction

Officers are instructed to preserve the following contradictions rather than resolve them prematurely.

Immog saved humanity from Cineric predation and taught humanity to harvest Cineric predation.

Immog opposed rulers who treated mankind as disposable and built systems that continue to dispose of mankind efficiently.

Immog refused to repeat whole-drop saturation and created distributed Draught regimes that allowed hereditary immortality hunger.

Immog relied on Proto-Undying Lords while fearing the machine aristocracy their descendants became.

Immog gave the poor a symbol of refusal and the nobles a substance by which refusal could be purchased.

Immog founded military continuity and created the problem of commanders who never leave.

Immog may have regretted what he made and did not, so far as authenticated public record proves, unmake it.

## Annex A — Extracts From the Disputed Muldunine Lineage

The following extracts are preserved in paraphrase because the complete text is not authorized for general restricted circulation. Translation variants are noted only where doctrinal consequences attach.

The Accord’s opening does not begin with triumph. It begins with inventory. Immog, or the writer using his name, lists the dead whose names he could still remember after the First Distillation and then admits that the list is incomplete. The omission is treated as sin, not error. This matters because early Legio heroic versions begin with the beast, the machine, and the golden drop. The Accord begins with forgotten soldiers. It therefore places the Founder’s guilt before the Founder’s miracle.

One passage describes the Cineric entity not as beast but as “a sovereignty of hunger wearing storm and bone.” Collegia Mortis commentators believe this wording indicates that the entity possessed organized selfhood rather than mere predatory instinct. Cultus Vitae censors prefer the term beast because citizens sleep better when enemies do not sound like nations.

Another passage describes the Proto-Undying engineers arguing over whether Immog’s body should be retrieved while still screaming. The final disputed extract reads: “If I return, question me. If I command without wound, question me twice. If I promise purity, kill me if you are still loyal.” This line appears in only three manuscript families and may be later Cohors Noctis insertion. It is nevertheless retained under seal because, forged or not, it has shaped several internal contingency protocols.

## Annex C — On Fractional Vitae in Devotional Materials

The Cultus Vitae continues to deny that ordinary parish candles contain actionable Draught. The denial is correct in the narrow technical sense that no approved public candle contains enough Cineric Vitae to produce noble-grade rejuvenation, military-grade restoration, or legal continuance entitlement.

The denial is misleading in the broader civil sense because trace sanctification practices, historical supply contamination, symbolic distillate dilution, chapel-grade medical overlap, and unauthorized local mixing have produced devotional materials containing fractional indices beyond zero. These quantities are usually too small to matter medically. They are not too small to matter socially.

The belief that a candle may contain a particle of the First Distillation generates behavior. Families hoard wax. Pilgrims burn entire wage packets in chapel alcoves. Cultus confessors are instructed not to promise healing, while also not striking hope from the hands of the dying unless doctrine requires cruelty in a documented manner.

A single true particle can ruin a thousand denials.

That is why the Concordia Logistica treats devotional distillate rumors as supply-chain issues, the Cultus treats them as pastoral hazards, the Collegia treats them as contamination data, and the poor treat them as Tuesday.

## Annex D — Immog in Military Oath Practice

Modern Legio oath practice descends from battlefield declarations attributed to the Marshaling. Recruits are not told the full historical uncertainty. They are told that Immog stood where death came through and did not yield. This is sufficient for basic courage and insufficient for command.

Senior officers are instructed that Immog’s example contains two commands in conflict. The first is to spend oneself before spending those entrusted to one’s care. The second is to remember that a commander whose body can return may become careless with bodies that cannot. This is why certain officer academies place cadets before a founder plate and require them to recite casualty rolls rather than heroic verses.

The oldest Legio oath variant states: “As Immog stood, I stand; as Immog rose, I rise; as Immog spent, I spend; as Immog erred, let my record be read.” The final clause is removed from most parade versions. It remains in black-school instruction and in units with unusually high survival discipline.

The Cohors Noctis reportedly uses a darker variant: “If Immog returns wrong, I do not kneel.” This is unconfirmed because asking Cohors personnel about oath liturgy is an efficient way to become a silence.

## Annex E — Industrial Memory After the Founder

The Great Distillation created an industrial memory problem that persists into the current era. Processes originally performed under direct founder authority became sanctified, classified, inherited, subcontracted, or hidden behind noble monopoly. As centuries passed, some houses remembered the ritual but not the reason, the warning but not the failure that produced it, the entitlement but not the emergency that justified it.

This pattern is visible across distillate stabilizer production, medical ampule licensing, high-noble body preparation, battlefield reconstitution rites, and anti-Reversion monitoring. A candle batch may be blessed over iron because the first apparatus was iron, though the blessing now reaches more auditors than metaphysics.

The Concord survives on such residues. Some are wisdom fossilized into habit. Some are mistakes fossilized into law. Telling the difference requires records, courage, and permission.

The Concord possesses records.

Courage and permission remain unevenly distributed.

## Annex F — Prohibited Speculation Regarding Immog’s Present State

The following speculative models are prohibited outside sealed review, but officers may encounter them in hostile doctrine, noble correspondence, machine-house theory, or black-market prophecy.

The **Dead Founder Model** asserts that Immog died permanently and that institutions conceal this to preserve legitimacy. This model is politically dangerous but metaphysically simple.

The **Hidden Founder Model** asserts that Immog survives in physical seclusion, wounded, transformed, imprisoned, sleeping, judging, or refusing return. This model produces cult rescue attempts and noble claimant fraud.

The **Fragmented Founder Model** asserts that Immog’s identity divided across successors, relics, Draught lineages, or institutional protocols. This model is favored by certain Collegia theorists and is intensely disliked by the Cultus.

The **Institutional Founder Model** asserts that Immog became the Concord itself through legal, mnemonic, doctrinal, and command-distributed continuity. This model is philosophically fashionable among dangerous clerks and therefore monitored.

The **Cineric Founder Model** asserts that Immog crossed partially into the Ashen Sea and remains as a human-shaped regnant force opposing or feeding upon Cineric entities. This model causes Liminal Choir disturbances when dramatized improperly.

The **False Founder Model** asserts that Immog never existed as described and that the First Distillation was later condensed into a person for governance purposes. This model is common among enemies and among graduate historians shortly before career termination.

None are authorized.

None are disproven in all sealed forms.

The safest working doctrine remains: Immog existed, Immog transformed, Immog founded, Immog vanished or ceased normal participation, and the institutions that speak in his name are responsible for their own orders regardless of what the Founder might have intended.
