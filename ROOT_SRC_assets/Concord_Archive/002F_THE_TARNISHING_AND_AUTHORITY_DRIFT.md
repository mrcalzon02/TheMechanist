# 002F — THE TARNISHING AND AUTHORITY DRIFT

**Cineric Concord Central Mortis Archive**  
**Record Family:** Expanded Hazard Dossier / Symbolic Corrosion of Office and Relic  
**Seal:** Ash-Black / Custodia Veritatis / Null Court Seal Registry / Ferrum Sanctum Material Authentication Office  
**Circulation:** Custodia seal inspectors, Null Court clerks, noble heralds, Legio relic wardens, Ferrum authenticity examiners, and Logistica officers authorized to validate ancient instruments of command.  
**Penalty for unauthorized recitation:** seal confiscation, title suspension, office erasure, or forced service as a witness against one’s own insignia.

This record is not maintained for comfort. It is maintained because the dead remember poorly, the living remember conveniently, and the Concord survives only when enough officers know which kind of memory is trying to enter the room.
## I. DEFINITION

The Tarnishing is Cineric degradation of repeated meaning. Its visible signs may be black film, green corrosion, dulling of gold, mirror failure, loss of engraved clarity, thermal wrongness, or unfamiliar script beneath official markings. Its true danger is authority drift. A tarnished thing may remain materially genuine while no longer obeying the office, memory, or law it was made to carry.

The public is told that tarnish means counterfeit relics. This is mercifully false. The most dangerous relics are often genuine. Their authenticity is the road by which the ash travels.

## II. OBJECTS MOST AT RISK

Items exposed to long ash pressure and repeated reverence are most vulnerable: medals, banners, seals, noble signets, fleet orders, death warrants, censure writs, heirloom weapons, regimental standards, court bells, saint masks, and documents signed by hands that have been dead too long to stop signing.

The Ashbourne love inheritance. The Cinerum loves repeated meaning. A seal stamped ten thousand times learns the shape of obedience. A banner carried through centuries of death becomes a vertical road. A noble signet worn by every heir in a line becomes a mouth through which the first and worst ancestor may attempt to speak.

## III. AUTHORITY DRIFT

A tarnished medal may commemorate not the battle recorded by the living but the battle remembered by the dead, wished by the enemy, or proposed by a Conceptual. A tarnished court seal may validate judgments under extinct law. A tarnished banner may rally troops toward a predecessor’s last mistake. A tarnished signet may recognize heirs already eaten from the registry. A tarnished weapon may fire at what its previous owner hated rather than what its current bearer aims at.

Several atrocities began because an ancient seal was trusted on the basis of age. Age is precisely what gave the ash time to learn its shape.

## IV. INSTITUTIONAL CONFLICT

The Ferrum Sanctum insists that machines can verify material authenticity. The Collegia Mortis replies that authenticity is not loyalty. The Null Court replies that a seal may be legally valid and metaphysically compromised at the same time. The Custodia Veritatis replies by confiscating the seal, arresting everyone who objected too eloquently, and sealing the argument until a safer century.

Noble houses resist tarnish inspections because inspections imply doubt, and doubt wounds inheritance. Legio relic wardens resist because banners are morale. Logistica resists because replacing seals disrupts supply. The Custodia resists none of these objections. It records them as symptoms.

## V. KNOWN CASES

The Black-Signed Muster of Albrax called three regiments to a battlefield abandoned ninety years prior; the regiments arrived to find their own memorial stones waiting. The Seventh Succession Bell of House Morvain rang once without striker or wind and legally disinherited every living claimant for fourteen minutes, during which time a dead infant was recognized as planetary governor. The Salt Writ of Orm validated ration confiscation under famine law repealed before the city existed. Each case was materially authentic. Each case killed people.

## VI. INSPECTION DOCTRINE

Authority-bearing objects are to be inspected for material authenticity, legal continuity, symbolic drift, memory pressure, and obedience alignment. An object that passes three categories and fails one remains dangerous. Officers are reminded that affection for relics is not evidence of safety. The dead may love an object as much as the living do. The difference is that the dead have fewer reasons to stop using it.

The Custodia maintains the right to confiscate, silence, melt, re-inscribe, or deny any authority-bearing object suspected of Tarnishing. Appeals may be filed after the object is neutralized. This order has prevented fewer complaints than one might hope and more disasters than the complainers deserve.
